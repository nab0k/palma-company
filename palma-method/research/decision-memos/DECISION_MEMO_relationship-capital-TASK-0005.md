## 1-2. Comparison: Five Capital Concepts

### Social Capital

- **Origin:** Sociology. Three competing founding definitions - Bourdieu (1986), Coleman (1988), Putnam (2000) - none of which fully agree with each other.
- **Level of analysis:** Contested by design. Bourdieu: individual/family. Coleman: relationship/structure. Putnam: community/society.
- **Strengths:** Deepest, most rigorously tested literature of the five. Decades of empirical replication across contexts. Gives Palma real intellectual weight to draw on.
- **Weaknesses:** Precisely because it has three competing definitions, "social capital" cannot be cited as if it means one thing. Any chapter that leans on it has to specify which register (Bourdieu's stratification lens, Coleman's structural-function lens, or Putnam's community-trust lens) it's using.
- **Overlap with Relationship Capital:** Substantial - this is Relationship Capital's deepest well. The overlap is the source of both terms' credibility and their confusion with each other.
- **Risk of adopting it directly (i.e., just calling Palma's concept "social capital"):** Would import three-way academic disagreement into an executive-facing book, and would misrepresent Relationship Capital as an unmediated restatement of existing theory rather than Palma's applied synthesis - already rejected in REVIEW_CLAUDE_0001's approved editorial decision.

### Relationship Capital

- **Origin:** No single canonical academic origin. Used loosely in management and marketing writing; Palma's own applied coinage, per the editorial position already approved in REVIEW_CLAUDE_0001.
- **Level of analysis:** Currently blurred (per REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002's specific critique) - the Chapter 6 v0.1 draft moves between leader-level, dyad-level, and organization-level without settling on one. This is the central problem this memo exists to help resolve.
- **Strengths:** Maximum flexibility to be defined for Palma's actual purpose (diagnosis, design, measurement) rather than inherited from a tradition built for different purposes. Business-facing, not academic-facing, vocabulary - easier for a client to hear and act on than "social capital."
- **Weaknesses:** Exactly because it has no external authority behind it, it is vulnerable to looking invented or unrigorous unless the book is unusually disciplined about defining and using it consistently. REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 flagged that the current working definition ("stock of trusted, reciprocal relationships") may exclude weak, latent, asymmetric, or institutionally mediated ties that should plausibly count.
- **Overlap:** By design, overlaps partially with all four other terms below - that overlap is not a flaw to eliminate, it's the reason a comparison table is useful at all.
- **Risk of adopting current wording as-is:** Locks in a definition that already has identified problems (unit of analysis, exclusion of latent/asymmetric ties) before those problems are fixed - the reason this memo exists.

### Relational Capital

- **Origin:** Intellectual-capital accounting literature (Edvinsson & Malone and related 1990s-2000s frameworks), where it is typically one of three components alongside human capital and structural capital.
- **Level of analysis:** The firm - specifically, the firm's stock of external relationship value (customers, suppliers, alliance partners) as an accounting/disclosure category.
- **Strengths:** Has an actual measurement tradition behind it (even an imperfect one) - accounting frameworks have tried to quantify it, unlike most social capital research, which mostly measures process rather than a monetizable stock.
- **Weaknesses:** Narrower literature than social capital, and less well known outside accounting/IC-management circles - citing it doesn't buy Palma the same instant credibility "social capital" would with a business reader who has encountered that term before. Not yet directly source-carded in this research pass - see Missing Evidence in the Chapter 6 packet.
- **Overlap with Relationship Capital:** High at the surface (same root word, similar target - firm-external relationships) but different in purpose: relational capital exists to be disclosed to investors; Relationship Capital exists to be diagnosed and designed by a leader.
- **Risk of adopting it directly:** Would tie Palma's concept to a specific, narrower accounting tradition and its assumptions about monetary quantifiability - a stronger claim about measurability than the book is currently prepared to defend (see Chapter 15/Measure discussion in the literature map).

### Stakeholder Capital

- **Origin:** No standard academic definition. Nearest real grounding is Edward Freeman's stakeholder theory (1984), which argues a firm's obligations extend beyond shareholders - but Freeman never coined a measurable "capital" on top of that argument.
- **Level of analysis:** The firm's relationship to its full stakeholder set (employees, customers, communities, regulators, shareholders) - broadest scope of the five terms.
- **Strengths:** Broadest, most inclusive framing - captures groups (regulators, communities) that "relationship capital" as currently drafted might underweight. Freeman's underlying theory is well-established and widely taught, giving real theoretical weight.
- **Weaknesses:** The word "capital" is doing work Freeman's own theory doesn't do - there is no established methodology for what "stakeholder capital" would actually measure, more so even than relational capital. REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 specifically flagged that this term "should not be dismissed solely because Freeman did not coin it" - a fair correction; the absence of a named coiner is not itself disqualifying.
- **Overlap with Relationship Capital:** Meaningful at the level of scope (both care about the full range of parties an organization depends on, not just customers or employees) but Stakeholder Capital is closer to a *governance* framing (whose interests count) than a *relationship-design* framing (how do you build and maintain the tie itself).
- **Risk of adopting it directly:** Risks defining Relationship Capital by *who* it concerns rather than *what kind of asset* it is - useful as a scoping check (does the definition capture regulators and communities, not just customers?) but not, on its own, a workable operational definition.

### Network Capital

- **Origin:** Sociology of mobilities - coined by John Urry (2007), elaborated with Jonas Larsen and Kay Axhausen (2006). New source card SC-023 added for this memo.
- **Level of analysis:** Individual or organizational capacity - specifically, the *capacity to sustain relationships across distance*, enabled by transport and communication infrastructure.
- **Strengths:** Precise and well-defined within its own scope; useful if Palma's concept needs to account for distributed, remote, or geographically dispersed stakeholder relationships specifically.
- **Weaknesses:** Materially narrower than what Palma needs - it is a theory about the *infrastructure that enables* relationship maintenance across distance, not a general theory of relationship value. A smaller, more specialized literature than any of the other four terms.
- **Overlap with Relationship Capital:** Low-to-moderate. The overlap is real but partial (both concern maintained relationships), and using the terms interchangeably would be a category error the way REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 warned against for other overclaims in this package.
- **Risk of adopting it directly:** Would import a specific, narrower academic claim (about distance and mobility) into a term meant to cover something broader - actively unhelpful if used as a synonym; only relevant if a specific sub-claim about distributed/remote relationships needs its own citation.

## 3-4. Three Candidate Executive Definitions of Relationship Capital

### Candidate A: The Reciprocity-Centered Definition (closest to the current Chapter 6 v0.1 wording)

> "Relationship Capital is the accumulated value of the trusted, reciprocal relationships an organization holds with the specific people and groups it depends on."

**What becomes easier:** Ties directly and cleanly to the trust and reciprocity research already built out in this package (Mayer/Davis/Schoorman, Gouldner, Zak) - Chapters 6 and 8 stay tightly linked and mutually reinforcing. Short, quotable, easy for an executive reader to hold in memory.

**What becomes harder:** This is the exact wording REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 flagged as too narrow - by requiring "trusted" and "reciprocal" as defining conditions, it structurally excludes weak ties (Chapter 7's central subject, which are valuable precisely because they are often low-trust and non-reciprocal in the short term), latent or dormant relationships (a former client who hasn't been in contact for years but could reactivate), and asymmetric or institutionally mediated relationships (a regulator, where reciprocity in Gouldner's sense doesn't straightforwardly apply). Using this definition would put Chapter 6 in quiet tension with Chapter 7's own argument.

**Chapters most affected:** Chapter 6 (defines the term), Chapter 8 (reinforced), Chapter 7 (tension - weak/non-reciprocal ties would sit awkwardly outside the definition), Chapter 10 (asymmetric/institutional relationships relevant to power-asymmetry critique would also sit awkwardly outside it).

### Candidate B: The Stock-and-Convertibility Definition (broader, addresses the review's specific critique)

> "Relationship Capital is an organization's accumulated stock of stakeholder relationships - active, latent, strong, and weak - together with the trust, structural position, and reciprocity norms that determine how readily that stock can be converted into support, information, or resources when needed."

**What becomes easier:** Directly resolves the review's core objection by explicitly including latent and weak ties, not just active/trusted/reciprocal ones. Separates the *stock* (the relationships that exist) from the *convertibility* (how usable that stock currently is) - which gives the book a natural way to distinguish relationship assets, network position, trust, and realized business value as related-but-different things, exactly the six-way distinction REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 asked Chapter 6 to make. Gives Chapters 6, 7, 8, and 10 a genuinely shared, non-contradictory vocabulary.

**What becomes harder:** It is a full sentence longer than Candidate A and less quotable as a single executive one-liner - it reads more like a working definition for internal use than a line a CEO would repeat in a meeting. It also risks becoming the kind of "vague capital" the Chapter 6 draft itself warned against - covering everything makes it harder to say precisely what would NOT count as Relationship Capital, and a definition with no clear boundary is hard to manage against.

**Chapters most affected:** All of Chapters 6 through 10, since this definition is built specifically to hold together the distinctions those chapters need to draw. Also affects the (not-yet-drafted) Chapter 9 on structural advantage, since "structural position" is now part of the definition itself.

### Candidate C: The Diagnostic-Asset Definition (narrowest, most operational)

> "Relationship Capital is the set of specific, identifiable relationships a leader can map, assess, and deliberately strengthen - the practical, actionable subset of an organization's broader relationship network worth managing as an asset."

**What becomes easier:** Maximally actionable and maximally honest about scope - it does not claim to be a comprehensive theory of all relationship value, only the portion an organization can actually see and act on. This sidesteps the unit-of-analysis problem entirely by making the definition self-scoping (it only ever refers to whatever a specific leader or team has actually mapped), and it plugs directly into the Five-Finger Model's Map/Design/Activate/Measure sequence without any translation needed.

**What becomes harder:** It is close to circular ("capital is what we choose to manage as capital"), which a skeptical academic-minded reader or reviewer will notice quickly. It also explicitly excludes the unmapped, unknown relationships that might hold real value but haven't been identified yet - which is in tension with Chapter 7's argument that the most valuable ties are often the ones an organization doesn't currently see. It is the weakest of the three candidates for grounding Chapter 6's theoretical credibility, even though it is the strongest for immediate practical use in later "Design" and "Diagnose" chapters.

**Chapters most affected:** Chapter 6 (defines the term, narrowly), Chapter 13 (Design) and Chapter 17 (Diagnose), where the operational framing pays off directly. Least well connected to Chapters 7, 8, and 10's theoretical arguments, which would need extra bridging language to stay consistent with a definition this narrow.

## 5. Recommendation

**Preferred: Candidate B, the Stock-and-Convertibility Definition.**

Reasoning: Candidate A is the definition REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 already identified as too narrow, and adopting it as-is would freeze in a known problem rather than fix it. Candidate C is the most immediately usable for the practice chapters but is too narrow and too close to circular to serve as the book's foundational definition - it would leave Chapters 7, 8, and 10 without a shared vocabulary and would invite exactly the "vague capital is not manageable capital" critique the Chapter 6 draft itself raised, just from the opposite direction (excluding real value rather than including too much). Candidate B is longer and less quotable, but it is the only one of the three that resolves the review's specific, concrete objection (exclusion of weak/latent/asymmetric ties) while still supporting the six-way distinction (relationship assets, network position, reputation, access, trust, realized business value) the review asked Chapter 6 to make explicit.

The length/quotability tradeoff does not need to be a final defect: a practical path is to adopt Candidate B as the book's precise working definition (used in Chapter 6's formal definitional passage and in any internal or Codex-facing documentation) while allowing a shorter, plain-language paraphrase to carry the idea in executive-facing contexts such as a LinkedIn post or a client conversation - for example, "the relationships your organization can call on when it needs to." That shorter phrase is not a competing fourth definition; it is a compressed restatement of Candidate B for a specific audience, and should be logged as such rather than drafted independently, to avoid the exact definitional drift this memo is trying to prevent.

This recommendation is not a rewrite of Chapter 6 and does not supersede the requirement, noted in REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 and the Chapter 6 packet itself, that the exact final wording receive Serhii's direct approval before it is used elsewhere in the manuscript or in any published material.
