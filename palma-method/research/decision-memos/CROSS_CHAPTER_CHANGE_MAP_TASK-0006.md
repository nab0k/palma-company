## Chapter 7 — Strong Ties, Weak Ties, and Bridges

**Terminology to change:** The chapter currently uses "strong ties" and "weak ties" as an informal binary (following Granovetter's own original phrasing). Once Chapter 6's dimensions are frozen, this chapter should explicitly map that binary onto two separate named dimensions: tie strength/frequency maps to **relationship strength**, while Burt's brokerage/structural-holes concept and the closure-vs-brokerage discussion map to **network structure**. This is a terminology alignment, not a conceptual change - the chapter's argument does not need to change, only its vocabulary.

**Claims affected:** The chapter's central claim ("strong ties and weak ties... perform different functions") remains intact, but should be reframed as an instance of the general point that Chapter 6 v0.2 now makes explicitly: relationship-level dimensions (relationship strength, trust, reciprocity, access) and portfolio-level dimensions (network structure) are independent and can move separately - a relationship can be weak on strength but strong on the network-structure value it contributes to the portfolio.

**Paragraphs likely to require revision:** The closing paragraph ("None of this argues for choosing bridging over closure, or weak ties over strong ones...") is the natural anchor point for the terminology update - it already makes the argument the new dimension names would formalize. The Barabási/hub paragraph ("There is a further layer worth introducing carefully here...") should be checked for consistency with Chapter 6 v0.2's Network Structure dimension description, which already carries the same VI-002 caveat.

**Dependencies on the final Relationship Capital decision:** Medium. Chapter 6 v0.2 already added a forward-looking paragraph naming this exact resolution ("This separation also resolves a tension that would otherwise sit quietly underneath the rest of this book..."); Chapter 7 will need a reciprocal cross-reference once both chapters are finalized together, ideally in the same revision wave.

## Chapter 8 — Trust, Status, and Reciprocity

**Terminology to change:** The most significant terminology dependency of the six chapters. "Trust" and "reciprocity" should be used consistently with their Chapter 6 v0.2 definitions (trust as a belief/ABI construct; reciprocity as a distinct normative exchange pattern, not a sub-mechanism of trust). The chapter's current framing ("Underneath the ABI model sits a second mechanism worth naming explicitly: trust isn't just a perception, it's an expectation built through repeated exchange...") blends the two in a way the new architecture asks to keep separate - this paragraph will need rewording to present trust and reciprocity as two independent Layer-2 dimensions rather than one nested inside the other.

**Claims affected:** The chapter's discussion of Google's Project Aristotle and Edmondson's psychological safety currently sits close to treating psychological safety as a form of trust. Chapter 6 v0.2 has pre-emptively added a paragraph explicitly separating trust (a dyadic, relationship-level belief) from psychological safety (a team-level, group construct), consistent with REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002's specific instruction not to merge the two. Chapter 8 should be revised to reference this distinction rather than re-deriving it, both to fix the conflation the review flagged and to reduce the duplication REVIEW_CLAUDE_0002 separately identified across chapters.

**Open architectural question - unresolved by this task:** "Status" appears in the chapter's title but is not one of the six named Layer-2 dimensions in the frozen architecture. Three options exist for a future revision, none selected here: (a) fold status into **access** (status may function as a predictor of whether access is granted); (b) fold status into **institutional reach** (status within formal/institutional structures); or (c) treat status as a Layer-4 **outcome** - a form of social recognition that results from accumulated Relationship Capital rather than a component of it. This is a real gap the six-dimension model does not currently resolve, and it should be raised explicitly with ChatGPT and Serhii rather than decided unilaterally here, since it may affect whether the chapter title itself needs to change.

**Paragraphs likely to require revision:** The ABI-model-plus-reciprocity paragraph (see above); the psychological-safety section, to reference rather than restate Chapter 6 v0.2's distinction; and the chapter's title/framing, pending resolution of the "status" question above.

**Dependencies on the final Relationship Capital decision:** High. This chapter cannot be finalized until (1) the trust/reciprocity separation is formally adopted and (2) the "status" architectural question is resolved.

## Chapter 10 — The Dark Side of Social Capital

**Terminology to change:** Uzzi's "over-embeddedness" finding should be explicitly reframed using the new dimension vocabulary: over-embeddedness is a case of **relationship strength** running high without corresponding **network structure** diversity - the chapter's existing embeddedness-curve argument already makes this point implicitly and would benefit from the explicit dimension names once frozen. Bourdieu's and Barabási's concentration-of-advantage discussion should likewise be explicitly tied to the **network structure** dimension rather than described only in general terms.

**Claims affected:** None of the chapter's core claims change substantively - the risks it documents (exclusion, over-embeddedness, concentration of advantage) map cleanly onto the new dimensions without requiring new argument, only relabeling.

**Paragraphs likely to require revision:** The Uzzi paragraph ("This directly complicates a recommendation the earlier chapters on strong ties and weak ties might otherwise leave a reader with...") is the natural anchor for the relationship-strength/network-structure relabeling. The closing Ostrom paragraph on participant voice and governance is the natural anchor for a new connection to **Objection 1** (the ownership/control risk of calling this asset "capital") from the companion definition package - Chapter 10 is the most natural home in the book for developing that objection further, since it already argues that relationship systems must not be designed as if the organization owns the people in them.

**Dependencies on the final Relationship Capital decision:** Medium-high. The relabeling itself is low-risk and can proceed once dimension names are frozen. The deeper dependency is on **Objection 5** from the definition package (the tension between "capital" framing and the book's consent-based ethics) - if ChatGPT and Serhii decide that objection requires a structural response rather than wording discipline alone, Chapter 10 is very likely where that response would need to be written, which would mean this chapter cannot be considered final until Objection 5's disposition is settled.
