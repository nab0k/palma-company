# Part III Published Case Candidates v0.1

This package contains one preferred published-case candidate per Chapter 11–16
and an alternate only where a material weakness requires one (Chapters 12 and
16). It is a draft for editorial review, not final publication copy. The cases
are not invented or composited, and this document does not resolve the example
placeholders in the v0.2 chapter packets.

## Chapter 11 (Listen) — Dell IdeaStorm

**Chapter fit:** Direct fit for Chapter 11's core claim that organizational listening is a distinct, buildable capability, not simply message dissemination, and for the chapter's response-capacity dimension specifically (does the organization have a real, not rhetorical, mechanism for heard input to change a decision).

**Mechanism:** In 2006, Dell launched IdeaStorm, a public platform where customers could submit and vote on product/service ideas, which Dell committed to reviewing and, in some cases, implementing. This created a visible, auditable link between expressed customer input and actual product decisions - directly testable against Chapter 11's response-capacity dimension.

**Public links:**
- Peer-reviewed academic study: Bayus, B.L. (2013). "Crowdsourcing New Product Ideas Over Time: An Analysis of the Dell IdeaStorm Community." *Management Science*, 59(1), 226-244. https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.1120.1599
- Kenan Institute of Private Enterprise summary: https://kenaninstitute.unc.edu/publication/crowdsourcing-new-product-ideas-over-time-an-analysis-of-the-dell-ideastorm-community/

**Evidence quality:** High for the existence and mechanics of the platform and its adoption pattern (peer-reviewed, published in a top operations/management journal). The specific figure "more than 400 ideas implemented since 2007" was found via secondary press/summary sources during this research pass and was not independently verified against a primary Dell disclosure - flag any specific implementation count as unverified until traced to a primary Dell source.

**Limitations:** The Bayus (2013) study's central finding is arguably a caution, not a success story: "serial ideators" who had one idea implemented became less likely to have a second idea implemented, and tended to submit less diverse, more self-referential ideas after their first success - a finding that complicates a simple "listening worked" narrative and should be represented accurately, not simplified into an uncomplicated success case.

**Copyright considerations:** The Management Science article itself is behind a subscription paywall (INFORMS); only the abstract/framing is freely accessible. No copyright barrier to describing the platform's existence and general mechanics, which are extensively covered in open press.

**Safe claims:** Dell created a real, public, response-linked listening channel in 2006; the channel has been studied academically; participation and implementation patterns are documented in peer-reviewed research; the same research found a specific, non-obvious limitation (diminishing returns from repeat contributors) worth including precisely because it complicates the simple story.

**Unsafe claims:** Any specific count of ideas implemented, dollar value generated, or causal claim that IdeaStorm alone changed Dell's product strategy - these require primary-source verification not completed in this pass.

**Decision: Use with qualification.** Strong primary/peer-reviewed grounding for the platform's existence and academic findings; unverified secondary figures must not be quoted without further sourcing.

## Chapter 12 (Map) — Schoder et al. (2008), "Analysis of Informal Communication Networks – A Case Study"

**Chapter fit:** Directly on point for Chapter 12's network-map and flow-map layers: a real, peer-reviewed case study of measuring informal communication networks inside an organization using an IT-based instrument ("Social Badges") rather than manual, error-prone mapping methods.

**Mechanism:** The study presents a case application of an automated sensor-based instrument for capturing personal interaction data more precisely than legacy (manual/survey) approaches, directly relevant to Chapter 12's caution that maps are built on incomplete data collected through a specific method with its own biases.

**Public links:**
- Schoder, D., et al. (2008/2009). "Analysis of Informal Communication Networks – A Case Study." *Business & Information Systems Engineering*, 1(2), 140-149. DOI: 10.1007/s12599-008-0018-z. Landing page: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12599-008-0018-z (marked "access: yes" on the publisher's own metadata at time of research, suggesting at least partial open access; full-text confirmation not completed in this pass).

**Evidence quality:** Medium-high. Peer-reviewed, named journal, verified DOI and abstract directly from the publisher. The case organization itself is not named in the abstract - consistent with the general pattern this research pass found across organizational network-mapping case studies (anonymization is standard practice in this literature, itself a finding worth stating plainly rather than treating as a shortcoming unique to this one source - see Part B of the accompanying evidence-repair document on why anonymization is the field's norm, not an exception).

**Limitations:** The underlying organization is not named in available materials; this is a case study of a measurement method more than a case study of a specific, identifiable organization's mapping journey, which is a different (and for this book, somewhat less narratively useful) kind of case than a named organizational story.

**Copyright considerations:** Publisher metadata suggests possible open access; full-text access was not confirmed in this pass and should be checked directly before any extended quotation is used in manuscript prose.

**Safe claims:** A real, peer-reviewed case study exists showing that automated, sensor-based network-mapping methods can outperform manual approaches in precision; this directly supports Chapter 12's discussion of method choice and incompleteness.

**Unsafe claims:** Any claim that names the underlying studied organization - it is not named in the materials obtained.

**Decision: Use with qualification.**

**Alternate (materially necessary — avoids over-reliance on an unnamed-organization case): Krackhardt & Hanson (1993), "Informal Networks: The Company Behind the Chart," *Harvard Business Review*.** This is already an existing, previously-verified source card in this project (SC-031) and is Chapter 12's own central applied technique. It is offered here as the alternate specifically because it is already established, already partially verified, and (like the Schoder case) also uses an illustrative/composite organizational example rather than one fully named real company - this is disclosed directly rather than presented as a limitation unique to the new candidate above. No new verification work was performed on SC-031 in this pass; it is cross-referenced, not re-verified.

## Chapter 13 (Design) — Google Project Aristotle

**Chapter fit:** Strong fit for Chapter 13's core claim that organizations can design conditions (in this case, team structures and norms) but cannot design the resulting social outcome (psychological safety, trust) directly - Project Aristotle is precisely a study of which designable conditions correlate with an outcome that itself cannot be mandated.

**Mechanism:** Google studied over 180 of its own internal teams to identify what distinguished high-performing teams; the widely-reported finding was that team composition mattered less than group norms, with psychological safety identified as the most significant factor.

**Public links:**
- Google's own official publication: "Understand team effectiveness," re:Work (Google's own public HR-research site). https://rework.withgoogle.com/intl/en/guides/understand-team-effectiveness
- Widely covered in mainstream press (e.g., Charles Duhigg, *New York Times Magazine*, 2016) - not independently re-verified in this pass but noted as the source of much of the concept's public visibility.

**Evidence quality:** High for the finding's existence and Google's own framing of it (published directly by Google, not merely reported secondhand). Medium for causal specificity - this is Google's own internal research, not independently peer-reviewed or replicated by outside researchers on Google's own data, a limitation worth stating plainly.

**Limitations - important, project-specific:** This project's own records (TASK_CLAUDE_0004, item "Verify Google Project Aristotle against primary materials," completed earlier in this project) indicate Project Aristotle was already researched and likely used as source material elsewhere in this book, plausibly in the trust/psychological-safety material associated with Chapter 8 or the existing Edmondson-grounded discussion (SC-013) that recurs throughout Chapters 11-16. **Using it again as Chapter 13's flagship published case risks a duplicate-case problem** the Chief Editor should resolve explicitly - either by confirming Chapter 8 does not already use it as a full case (only as a supporting citation), or by choosing a different case for one of the two chapters.

**Copyright considerations:** Google's own re:Work material is published for public use; no paywall.

**Safe claims:** Google conducted a large-scale internal study; psychological safety was identified as the leading factor; Google itself has published this finding on its own public site.

**Unsafe claims:** Presenting the five-factor model as independently, externally peer-reviewed and replicated at the same evidentiary standard as, e.g., Edmondson's original academic research - it is Google's own internal analysis, reported by Google.

**Decision: Use with qualification**, specifically flagging the duplicate-case risk with Chapter 8 for editorial resolution before this candidate is adopted.

## Chapter 14 (Activate / Intervene Under Uncertainty) — Adobe Kickbox

**Chapter fit:** Strong, close fit. Kickbox is a real, structured, safe-to-fail intervention mechanism (a literal box containing a prepaid card and a staged process) explicitly designed around limited, low-cost, reversible experiments - matching Chapter 14's central architecture (limited intervention, sensing, response) almost component-for-component.

**Mechanism:** Adobe gives employees a "red box" containing a $1,000 prepaid card and a staged innovation process; ideas that show promise progress to further investment stages ("blue box"); the program is explicitly designed on the premise that most ideas will fail and that this is acceptable given the low cost of each individual experiment.

**Public links:**
- Academic case study: Dann, J. (USC Marshall / Lloyd Greif Center for Entrepreneurial Studies), "'Kickboxing' at Adobe Systems," distributed via The Case Centre: https://www.thecasecentre.org/products/view?id=135275 (multiple editions: 2015, 2021, 2023, per Greif Center records).
- Public press: Forbes, "Adobe Kickbox Gives Employees $1000 Credit Cards And Freedom to Pursue Ideas" (2015). https://www.forbes.com/sites/mzhang/2015/08/19/adobe-kickbox-gives-employees-1000-credit-cards-and-freedom-to-pursue-ideas/
- Program's own public materials: https://kickbox.org/

**Evidence quality:** High for the program's existence and mechanics (multiple independent public sources agree); medium for outcome statistics (a commonly repeated figure - roughly 6 of 1000 early participants progressing through all stages - was found via secondary/press sources and was not independently traced to a primary Adobe disclosure in this pass).

**Limitations:** The full academic case study (Dann) is a licensed teaching case distributed through The Case Centre, a case-study clearinghouse - the complete case text is not freely accessible and would need to be purchased/licensed for any use beyond general public description.

**Copyright considerations:** The Case Centre case is commercially licensed (standard for business-school teaching cases); public press coverage and Adobe's/Kickbox's own public materials are freely usable for general description; any direct, extended quotation from the licensed case text would require permission.

**Safe claims:** Adobe created a real, structured, low-cost, safe-to-fail innovation program in 2013; it is documented in an academic teaching case and extensive public press; its design (small bounded risk, staged progression) matches Chapter 14's architecture closely.

**Unsafe claims:** Specific participation/success statistics beyond what can be traced to a primary Adobe source; implying the licensed academic case's full analytical content (which was not obtained in this pass) without separately securing it.

**Decision: Use with qualification.**

## Chapter 15 (Measure) — Enterprise Rent-A-Car / Reichheld (2003)

**Chapter fit:** Directly relevant, but requires careful framing given Chapter 15's central caution against composite/universal scoring. This case is best used as an example of a *well-scoped single leading indicator tied to one specific, stated decision* - not as validation that a single number can represent multi-dimensional relationship health generally.

**Mechanism:** Enterprise Rent-A-Car (under then-CEO Andy Taylor) used a simple, two-question survey to rank individual branch offices, tying branch-manager evaluation and advancement decisions specifically to the top-box score - a real, named, decision-linked use of a simple metric, which Fred Reichheld used as his central illustrative case in introducing what became the Net Promoter Score.

**Public links:**
- Reichheld, F.F. (2003). "The One Number You Need to Grow." *Harvard Business Review*, 81(12), 46-55. https://hbr.org/2003/12/the-one-number-you-need-to-grow

**Evidence quality:** High for the case's existence and framing (primary HBR source, real named company and named CEO, not a secondary retelling). This source (Reichheld) already has an existing, previously-verified card in this project (SC-026, Reichheld & Sasser retention economics) - this is Reichheld's later, related but distinct work, not a re-verification of SC-026 itself.

**Limitations - important:** This case's own logic sits in some tension with Chapter 15 v0.2's central argument. Reichheld's article promotes a single number as sufficient for growth prediction generally; Chapter 15 explicitly and permanently declines to endorse any single universal score. **This case should be used specifically to show the value of one well-scoped metric tied to one specific, named decision (branch-manager evaluation) - not as an endorsement of single-number measurement as a general practice.** This distinction must be stated explicitly if this case is adopted, or the case will contradict the chapter's own argument.

**Copyright considerations:** HBR article is paywalled for full-text access; the core finding and Enterprise/Andy Taylor framing are extensively covered in secondary, freely accessible sources.

**Safe claims:** Enterprise Rent-A-Car used a specific, simple, decision-linked metric for a specific, stated purpose (branch ranking tied to manager evaluation); this is real, named, and primary-sourced (HBR, 2003).

**Unsafe claims:** Using this case to imply single-number measurement is validated as sufficient for assessing multi-dimensional relationship health generally - this would directly contradict Chapter 15's own central, required argument and must not be the framing adopted.

**Decision: Use with qualification**, with the framing constraint above stated as a condition of use, not an optional caveat.

## Chapter 16 (Connect) — LEGO Ideas

**Chapter fit:** Strong fit for Chapter 16's central v0.2 argument about member-to-member value creation and the communication/community distinction: LEGO Ideas is a platform where community members do not just communicate with LEGO, they create value for each other (proposing, refining, and voting on each other's product concepts) which LEGO then selectively converts into commercial products with (per public reporting) creator recognition.

**Mechanism:** Originally launched as LEGO Cuusoo (2008, via partnership with Japan's CUUSOO System) and relaunched as LEGO Ideas (2014); community members submit product concepts, other members vote, and ideas crossing a support threshold are formally reviewed by LEGO for production, with the originating member credited.

**Public links:**
- Academic case study: "Innovation management based on proactive engagement of customers: A case study on LEGO Group. Part II," ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283548425 (2015).
- Platform itself (public, ongoing): https://ideas.lego.com

**Evidence quality:** Medium-high. The platform's existence, mechanics, and general business outcomes (approximately 10 community-originated sets launched as official products per year, per secondary summary sources) are well corroborated across multiple independent sources; the specific academic case study's full text was not independently obtained in this pass (found via search result summary only).

**Limitations:** The "approximately 10 sets per year" and "development time reduced up to 4×" figures were found via secondary/summary sources (not the primary academic case text itself) and should be treated as directionally credible but not independently verified to primary-source precision.

**Copyright considerations:** General platform description and business-press coverage are freely usable; the specific academic case study should be obtained and read in full before any detailed quotation.

**Safe claims:** LEGO operates a real, long-running (2008-2014-present across two platform generations), publicly documented member-to-member value-creation platform with a formal, credited path from community proposal to commercial product.

**Unsafe claims:** Specific production-time-reduction or annual-product-count figures beyond what can be traced to a primary LEGO or fully-read academic source.

**Decision: Use with qualification.**

**Alternate (materially necessary — avoids sole reliance on an unread-in-full academic source, and because a stronger-standing candidate already exists in this project): Harley-Davidson Owners Group (HOG).** This is already an existing, previously-established source card in this project (SC-021, McAlexander/Harley-Davidson) and was already used as a counterexample in Chapter 16 v0.1 and v0.2. It is offered here as the alternate specifically because it is already verified and already integrated into Chapter 16's existing argument (the design-vs-control distinction, per Chapter 13/16 v0.2's discussion) - adopting it as the chapter's primary published-case candidate as well would avoid introducing a new, less-verified source (LEGO Ideas) while the existing one remains available and already load-bearing. No new verification work was performed on SC-021 in this pass; it is cross-referenced, not re-verified.
