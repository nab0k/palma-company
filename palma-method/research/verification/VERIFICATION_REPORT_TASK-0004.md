## 1. The 50/76/40% trust statistic - RESOLVED

**Primary source found:** Paul J. Zak, "The Neuroscience of Trust," *Harvard Business Review*, January-February 2017, pp. 84-90. (hbr.org/2017/01/the-neuroscience-of-trust) — now Source Card SC-018.

**What was confirmed:** Zak's HBR article reports that, compared with people at low-trust companies, people at high-trust companies show 74% less stress, 106% more energy at work, 50% higher productivity, 13% fewer sick days, 76% more engagement, 29% more life satisfaction, and 40% less burnout. The 50/76/40 figures previously in circulation are a genuine subset of this seven-number finding, not a fabrication or misattribution.

**Caveats to carry into manuscript use:** The underlying research is self-report survey data (Zak's proprietary "Ofactor" instrument) compared across trust quartiles, not a controlled causal experiment - correlational, not proof of causation. The HBR article itself does not publish full sample size or significance testing. Manuscript prose should attribute the figures specifically to Zak (2017) and should either cite the full seven-statistic set or note explicitly that only a subset is being used.

**Claims-ledger status:** VI-004 changed from `open` (blocking, high severity) to `resolved`.

## 2. Ostrom's eight design principles - STRENGTHENED, NOT FULLY RESOLVED

**What was done:** The original 1990 Cambridge University Press text (*Governing the Commons*) remains access-restricted on the Internet Archive (borrow-only, not full-text searchable). In lieu of direct page access, the eight principles were triangulated across five independent secondary/tertiary academic sources total (two from the original research pass - P2P Foundation Wiki, Agrarian Trust - plus three added for this task: the Ostrom Workshop at Indiana University, the Dartmouth SESMAD case database, and a direct Chapter 3 paraphrase from a further academic-adjacent source).

**What was confirmed:** All five sources agree on the same eight principles and on the structural detail that seven are drawn from Ostrom's Chapter 3 analysis of four core long-enduring commons cases, with "nested enterprises" functioning as an eighth principle specifically for larger or nested resource-governance systems - a nuance that had been under-specified in the original card.

**What remains unverified:** No direct page-number citation to the original 1990 text has been confirmed. This claim should be classified as **strengthened, not resolved** and cited in the manuscript without a specific page number (e.g., "Ostrom, *Governing the Commons*, 1990, Chapter 3" rather than a page-pinned quotation) until institutional library access allows a direct page check.

**Claims-ledger status:** VI-001 changed from `open` to `strengthened_not_resolved`. Remains flagged; must not be marked fully verified, per the explicit constraint carried over from TASK_CLAUDE_0003's Verification Flags.

## 3. Google Project Aristotle - RESOLVED

**Primary source found:** Google re:Work, "Understand Team Effectiveness" (rework.withgoogle.com/intl/en/guides/understand-team-effectiveness) — now Source Card SC-020.

**What was confirmed:** Fetched and read directly. Google's People Analytics team studied 180 teams (115 engineering, 65 sales) using 35+ statistical models. In order of importance, the five dynamics that predicted team effectiveness were: psychological safety, dependability, structure and clarity, meaning, and impact. Psychological safety was explicitly the top-ranked factor. The page directly credits and quotes Amy Edmondson's (SC-013) "team psychological safety" construct as foundational to the study's framing.

**Caveats to carry into manuscript use:** This is Google's own first-party corporate research, published on its own site, not independently peer-reviewed or replicated externally. Full statistical detail (effect sizes, significance) is not published. Manuscript prose should attribute the finding explicitly as "Google's own internal People Analytics research" rather than as an externally validated academic law, and should link it explicitly back to Edmondson's underlying academic construct.

**Claims-ledger status:** VI-003 changed from `open` (medium severity) to `resolved`.
