### Verbatim abstract (primary source, SAGE Journals)

> "Building on Benedict Anderson's concept of 'imagined communities,' we apply the term to a class of social locations that we term *wispy communities*. By *wispy communities*, we refer to social ties that exist within evanescent, limited micro-publics—worlds of action that are temporary, limited in time and space, and have the potential of being displaced by more insistent identities. We divide occasions into events and gatherings, and then divide gatherings into macro-groups ('nations,' such as Woodstock or Burning Man) and micro-groups ('fests,' including focused groups, such as the Lebowski Fest). A wispy community is a distinctive form of local affiliation that underscores both the communal features of occasions and their evanescent quality. In such communities relations are frequently intense, but after their conclusion relations slip into latent memory, available to be retrieved under proper circumstances."

This is materially more precise than the paraphrase used in `SOCIOLOGY_OF_MICROCOMMUNITIES_RESEARCH_v0.1.md` and the Chapter 12/16 v0.2 packets. Specifically, three elements were not previously captured and should be treated as corrections/additions, not replacements, of the existing paraphrase:

1. The theoretical lineage is explicit: the concept is built directly on **Benedict Anderson's "imagined communities"** (Anderson, B. 1983. *Imagined Communities*. London: Verso - confirmed present in the article's own reference list). Prior project material did not name this lineage.
2. The article proposes a specific internal typology: **occasions** divide into **events** and **gatherings**; **gatherings** further divide into **macro-groups** ("nations" - large, diffuse occasions like Woodstock or Burning Man) and **micro-groups** ("fests" - smaller, more focused gatherings, with the Lebowski Fest given as the named example). This typology was not previously captured at this level of detail.
3. The mechanism for what happens after a wispy community's occasion ends is specific: relations do not simply dissolve; they **"slip into latent memory, available to be retrieved under proper circumstances."** This is a more precise and more usable claim than "relations slip into latent memory" as a general gloss - it implies wispy-community ties are dormant, not extinguished, and can be reactivated, which is a materially different claim than simple transience.

## Part B — Ethical evidence for organizational/social-network mapping

### Primary sources located and read in full

**1. Borgatti, S.P., and Molina, J.-L. (2005). "Toward ethical guidelines for network research in organizations." *Social Networks*, 27(2), 107-117. DOI: 10.1016/j.socnet.2005.01.004.**

Full text obtained and read in full (author-hosted PDF via ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Stephen-Borgatti/publication/228648411). This is a genuine primary source, not a secondary summary - the complete article, including its two model consent-document appendices, was retrieved and read.

Core findings, stated precisely (evidence, not Palma recommendation):

- Organizational network research structurally involves **three parties** (researcher, respondent, and the organization/management), unlike ordinary survey research's two parties (researcher, respondent) - this is the article's foundational distinction and the source of most of the ethical complications that follow.
- **Anonymity is structurally unavailable** in network data collection at the collection stage, because "the collection of names of either individuals or social units is not incidental to the research but its very point" (this specific framing is confirmed independently in Kadushin's work, below, suggesting it is a shared, field-level observation, not one author's idiosyncratic claim). What can be offered instead is **anonymization after the fact** (replacing names with codes before publication) - not the same protection, and not available at all in reports given to management, since a network report is far less useful to management with names removed.
- **Non-response does not guarantee omission.** A person who declines to participate in a network survey can still appear in the resulting map, because other respondents may name them. Borgatti and Molina document a real example from their own consulting practice: a respondent who refused to participate was nonetheless named by multiple others in response to a "who do you have conflicts with" question, and her attempt to opt out did not prevent that conclusion from being drawn.
- **The "mixed academic/consulting" case is a specific, named danger.** When a researcher exchanges a report to management for research access (a common arrangement), management may use the network map to make personnel decisions - including firing "non-central" workers - a use the original respondents frequently do not anticipate or consent to.
- The article proposes two concrete instruments, provided as reusable templates in its appendices: a **Management Disclosure Contract (MDC)** - a written agreement between researcher and organization stating exactly what data management will see and how it may be used - and a **Truly Informed Consent (TIC) form**, which requires that respondents see the MDC itself and an example of the actual network-map output before consenting, not just a generic description of the study.
- Additional concrete, evidence-grounded design recommendations: anonymization/aggregation of anything shown to management wherever possible; uncoerced participation (researcher, not management, solicits participation); "respondent give-back" (individualized feedback to each participant about their own network position, framed as converting an otherwise-extractive exchange into a more equitable one); a **true opt-out option** (a person's data is fully excluded from all analyses, not merely anonymized); and **pre-survey consent** (building the roster-style questionnaire only from names of people who already agreed to participate, so that a missing name does not visibly signal refusal).

**2. Borgatti, S.P., and Molina, J.-L. (2003). "Ethical and Strategic Issues in Organizational Social Network Analysis." *Journal of Applied Behavioral Science*, 39(3), 337-349.**

The companion, earlier article by the same authors (confirmed via the 2005 article's own reference list, which cites it directly, and via independent secondary confirmation). Full text not independently obtained in this pass (only abstract-level material was retrieved: the top three ethical concerns identified are described as violation of privacy, psychological harm, and harm to individual standing). Logged as **use with qualification** - the 2005 article substantially supersedes and extends this one and was read in full, so this earlier piece is cited here only as corroborating context, not as an independently verified primary source in its own right.

**3. Kadushin, C. (2005). "Who benefits from network analysis: ethics of social network research." *Social Networks*, 27(2), 139-153.**

Published in the same *Social Networks* special issue (27:2, 2005) as the Borgatti and Molina piece immediately above - confirming this was a coordinated, field-level effort to establish ethical guidance for network research at a specific point in the field's development. Only abstract-level material was retrieved (not the full text): the article's central, empirically-grounded conclusion is that **"academic researchers always benefit, organizations, society and science may benefit, but individual respondents rarely do"** - a direct, named asymmetry-of-benefit finding. Kadushin independently confirms the same "collecting names is the point, not incidental" structural problem identified by Borgatti and Molina. Logged as **use with qualification** (abstract-level only, not full text).

**4. Kadushin, C. *Understanding Social Networks: Theories, Concepts, and Findings* (Oxford University Press, 2012), Chapter 11 (ethics).**

Referenced via secondary review sources (a book review and course-syllabus-adjacent material), not read directly. Logged as **defer** - a real, citable, authoritative book exists, but this pass did not obtain or read the actual chapter text, and no claim from it is used in this package beyond noting its existence as a further resource.

### Regulatory/legal source (distinct evidence type from academic research)

**5. General Data Protection Regulation (EU) 2016/679, Article 5(1)(c) — the data minimization principle.**

Retrieved from a primary regulatory-text hosting source (gdpr-info.eu, which republishes the official regulation text) and corroborated by the UK Information Commissioner's Office (ico.org.uk) guidance. ## Part C — Group-level measurement: microgroup durability, role transfer/succession, participation concentration, founder dependence

Per this task's explicit instruction, this section does not propose or imply a validated composite instrument. Each construct is treated separately, with real findings reported honestly, including where the honest finding is negative (no validated instrument exists).

### Participation concentration

**Real, validated, quantitative measures exist** for concentration generally, though not purpose-built for "community health" diagnosis:

- **Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):** a standard concentration measure, most established in economics (market concentration) and directly transferable in mathematical form to participation concentration (sum of squared shares of total participation held by each individual). Well-established, real, widely used - but its use for diagnosing relationship-portfolio or community concentration specifically is this project's own extension of a measure built for a different original purpose (market share), not something the HHI literature itself validates for this use case.
- **Normalized degree/betweenness/closeness centralization measures**, as used in social network analysis (confirmed via a 2026 peer-reviewed methods paper, "Evaluating global measures of network centralization: Axiomatic and numerical assessments," PMC12875492, which recommends using multiple centralization measures jointly since each captures a different aspect of concentration and no single measure is sufficient alone). This is real, current, peer-reviewed methodological guidance directly on point for measuring concentration in a mapped network - directly relevant to Chapter 12's network map layer and Chapter 15's measurement discussion.

**Use with qualification:** both are real, established, quantitative tools. Neither was designed for, or validated on, organizational relationship or community data specifically - applying them there is a reasonable, common extension (already implicitly done throughout applied social network analysis) but not itself independently validated for that specific use.

### Microgroup durability

**Real, relevant academic grounding exists but does not directly answer "how long do microgroups last":**

- McPherson, M. (1983). "An Ecology of Affiliation." *American Sociological Review*, 48, 519-532, and the related "Blau space" research program. This work models voluntary-association membership duration using a competitive-ecology framework: members leave associations fastest when they are atypical of the group ("niche edge") or when competing groups pull for the same members ("niche overlap") - both hypotheses reported as empirically supported in the literature reviewed. This is real, well-established, primary sociological theory - but it models **individual membership duration within a competitive population of groups**, not the survival or persistence of a specific bounded microgroup as a unit, which is the more specific question Chapter 15 v0.2 raises.
- Shi, Y., Dokshin, F.A., Genkin, M., and Brashears, M.E. (2017). "A Member Saved Is a Member Earned? The Recruitment-Retention Trade-Off and Organizational Strategies for Membership Growth." A modern, quantitative study of the same general territory (membership growth and retention dynamics), confirmed to exist via its DOI record (10.1177/0003122417693616) but not read in full in this pass.

**Negative finding, stated directly:** no source located in this pass provides a validated, purpose-built instrument for measuring "microgroup durability" as this project's Chapter 15 defines it (whether a specific, bounded microgroup identified through mapping still exists in a meaningful sense at a later point in time). The closest available grounding (McPherson's ecology of affiliation) answers an adjacent but different question (why individuals leave voluntary associations in a competitive field), not the specific question this book's Chapter 12/15 material raises.

### Role transfer / succession

**Real research exists, is largely practitioner-oriented or qualitative, and confirms the negative finding is itself well-documented:**

- Multiple sources (a Waldenu doctoral dissertation on nonprofit succession-planning strategies; an academia.edu-hosted paper on nonprofit executive succession planning and organizational sustainability; an Emerald Publishing practitioner article specifically on nonprofit founder succession) converge on the same, well-corroborated finding: **succession planning is widely acknowledged as important by nonprofit and voluntary-sector leaders, but is frequently absent in practice, and is more often handled reactively (after a transition has already begun) than proactively.** This is itself a real, useful, if modest, finding - and it is a negative finding about instrumentation and practice, not a positive validated measurement method.
- One study used Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis (a qualitative method examining how founders themselves make sense of succession) - real, methodologically legitimate, but explicitly not a quantitative, replicable instrument.
- A distinct, more quantifiable body of research exists in software engineering under the term **"bus factor"** - the minimum number of key contributors whose sudden unavailability would critically impair a project, most rigorously studied in open-source software contexts. A cited empirical study (referenced across multiple arXiv papers found in this pass) examined 133 popular open-source projects and found 65% had a bus factor of 2 or lower. This is a real, quantifiable, empirically-studied measure of key-person dependency - but its domain is software contribution (measurable via commit/code-contribution data), and its transfer to general organizational or community role-transfer/succession is an extension this project would be making, not one the bus-factor literature itself validates.

**Negative finding, stated directly:** no validated, general-purpose instrument for measuring "role transfer success" in organizational or community microgroups (as opposed to succession planning as a qualitative practice, or bus factor as a software-specific quantitative measure) was located in this pass.

### Founder dependence

Covered substantially by the role-transfer/succession research above (nonprofit-sector succession literature) and the bus-factor literature (software-sector key-person dependency). **No single instrument bridges both domains or generalizes to organizational communities/microgroups generally.** This is logged as the same negative finding stated twice from two different angles: the literature confirms founder/key-person dependence is a real, widely-recognized, and under-measured problem, in two different fields, using two different methods, neither of which has been validated for the general organizational-microgroup case this book's Chapter 15 addresses.

### Explicit refusal to invent, per this task's instruction

This package does not propose a validated instrument combining participation concentration, microgroup durability, role transfer, and founder dependence into any single measure, composite, or Relationship Capital score. Where real, usable measures exist (HHI, network centralization measures, bus factor), they are reported as what they are: real, validated for their original domain, and an extension - not a validation - when applied to this book's subject matter. Where no real measure exists (microgroup durability as a bounded-unit-survival question; role transfer as a general organizational construct), that absence is reported directly as a negative finding, per this task's explicit instruction, rather than filled with an invented substitute.
