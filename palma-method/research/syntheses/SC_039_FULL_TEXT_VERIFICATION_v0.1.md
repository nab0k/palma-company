## 4. Conceptual method (as stated by the article)

This is a theoretical/conceptual sociology article, not an empirical study with a single dataset. Its method is a typological synthesis: the authors build a classification of transient social forms, grounding the framework explicitly in Benedict Anderson's concept of "imagined communities" (Anderson, 1983), which they extend from the nation-state level down to what they call a "mesolevel." The typology is illustrated throughout with named examples drawn from Fine's own prior ethnographic fieldwork and from documented secondary sources (cited in the reference list) rather than from a new, dedicated empirical study conducted for this article.

## 5. The article's actual typology (verified against full text)

- **Club form:** ongoing, scheduled "meetings" with an assumed core group and continuity across sessions, even if individual attendance oscillates. Named examples in the article: a weekly poker game, a chess club, a dramatic society, a fantasy-gaming group, the Sherlockian "Baker Street Irregulars," and Fine's own earlier fieldwork on a mushroom-collecting society.
- **Occasion form:** rare, memorable, temporally bounded assemblies, contrasted with the club form's routine scheduling. Exact quote, page 8: **"We divide the occasion form into two subtypes: the event and the gathering."**
  - **Event:** a brief occasion organized around a performance, in which attendees do not expect to develop lasting ties with one another (their attention and investment is directed at the performance/performers, not at each other).
  - **Gathering:** liminal, less frequent than routine club meetings, organized around a shared interest, generating what the article calls "interactional glue." After the gathering ends, the associated identity and ties "slip into latent memory, available to be retrieved under proper circumstances" if the gathering recurs.
- Gatherings are further divided by scale and institutional control into:
  - **"Nations" (macro-gatherings):** Woodstock (August 1969, Bethel, NY, approximately 500,000 attendees) and Burning Man (Black Rock Desert, NV, since 1986, approximately 50,000 participants by 2009).
  - **"Fests" (micro-gatherings):** Lebowski Fest (a fan gathering for the film *The Big Lebowski*, begun in 2002 in Louisville, KY, held in 14 cities by 2009, citing unpublished research by Ann Jamison), organized chess tournaments (citing Puddephat 2005, 2008), and Fine's own prior fieldwork on folk-art collectors, chess players, mushroomers, and fantasy gamers.

**These are the article's actual cases.** None of the previously-recorded SC-039 example cases — queues, temporary online threads, or anonymous/semi-anonymous gatherings — appear anywhere in the full text. That prior card content was in error and has been corrected.

## 6. Article's own conclusion (verbatim close, page 14, lightly paraphrased for length per copyright limits)

The article closes by generalizing beyond nations to other "emotional anchors of many sizes, shapes, and moments," suggesting these "tiny publics have the potential for revealing that, in truth, we belong together." This is the article's own closing framing; it is a claim about identity and belonging in leisure/fan-culture contexts, not an organizational or managerial recommendation of any kind.

## 7. What the article does NOT contain

- No organizational, workplace, or business case of any kind. All named examples are leisure, hobbyist, or fan-culture gatherings (poker, chess, mushroom-collecting, fantasy gaming, Lebowski Fest, Woodstock, Burning Man).
- No discussion of organizational network mapping, internal communication channels, Slack/digital-workplace tools, or any business context.
- No claim, stated or implied, that organizations should avoid treating internal groups as maturing toward "community" — this is exclusively a Palma-book application of the typology, not a conclusion the authors draw or would necessarily endorse.
- No quantitative measurement instrument, index, or composite score of any kind — consistent with this project's TASK_CLAUDE_0014 negative finding on group-level measurement instruments generally.
