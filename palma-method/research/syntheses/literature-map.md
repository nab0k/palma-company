## 1. What is social capital?

There is no single agreed definition of social capital in the literature - the field has three founding definitions that pull in genuinely different directions, and the book needs to take a position rather than blend them silently (see the companion note on conflicting definitions).

Bourdieu (SC-001) defines it as an individual or family asset: "the aggregate of the actual or potential resources which are linked to possession of a durable network of more or less institutionalised relationships." For Bourdieu, social capital is convertible into economic capital, unevenly distributed, and bound up with class reproduction - it is fundamentally about advantage, not cooperation for its own sake.

Coleman (SC-002) defines it functionally rather than substantively: "social capital is defined by its function... it consists of some aspect of a social structure, and facilitates certain actions of individuals who are within the structure." Coleman treats it as closer to a public good - it inheres in the relationship, not in either party alone, and benefits everyone embedded in the structure (not just the person who "built" it).

Putnam (SC-003) scales the concept up to the community/societal level: "connections among individuals - social networks and the norms of reciprocity and trustworthiness that arise from them." His bonding/bridging distinction ("sociological superglue" versus "sociological WD-40") has become the most widely used practical vocabulary in the field and is directly usable in the book.

The throughline across all three: social capital is not the network itself but the *value the network structure makes possible* - access, information, support, or enforceable trust that would not exist for an isolated individual. Where the three disagree is on unit of analysis (individual vs. relationship vs. community) and on whether it should be understood primarily as a private advantage or a collective good.

## 2. What is relationship capital? How does it differ from social capital?

"Relationship capital" is not a term with a single canonical academic origin comparable to Bourdieu's or Coleman's social capital - it appears more often in management, marketing, and intellectual-capital literature (e.g., discussions of "customer capital" and "relational capital" as a third leg alongside human capital and structural capital in intellectual-capital accounting frameworks) than in foundational sociology. This is a genuine gap the book will need to resolve explicitly rather than assume: either (a) treat "relationship capital" as Palma's own applied, executive-facing term for social capital operationalized at the level of a specific relationship or organization, and say so plainly, or (b) anchor it to the existing intellectual-capital literature (Edvinsson & Malone and related work on relational capital) and cite that lineage. Presenting "relationship capital" as if it were an established term with the same pedigree as social capital, without this disambiguation, would violate the book's own Evidence Standards (Section 24). This is flagged as Open Question #1 in the companion missing-theories note and should be resolved as a Decision Log entry before Chapter 6 is drafted.

Working distinction proposed for editorial review: social capital (academic term) describes the general phenomenon of value embedded in social structure; relationship capital (Palma's term) is the applied, diagnostic, and design-oriented version aimed at leaders - measurable, mappable, and buildable inside an organization's specific stakeholder system, rather than a society-wide public good.

## 3. What forms of value emerge from networks and relationships?

The literature identifies at least four distinct mechanisms, and the book should keep them distinct rather than collapsing them into one vague "relationships are good" claim:

**Closure value** (Coleman, SC-002): dense, mutually-connected networks enable trust, norm enforcement, and information reliability. Coleman's dropout-rate finding shows this operating independent of individual family resources.

**Bridging/brokerage value** (Granovetter SC-004, Burt SC-005): ties that span otherwise disconnected clusters deliver novel information, earlier access, and control over information flow. Burt's 2004 finding that brokerage position predicted managers' idea ratings, compensation, and promotion - independent of individual intelligence - is the strongest quantified evidence in the literature map for this mechanism.

**Structural/positional value** (Barabási SC-012): position within an emergent network structure (hub vs. periphery) compounds over time through preferential attachment, independent of either closure or brokerage logic.

**Collective-action value** (Ostrom SC-006, against Olson SC-011): well-designed group institutions can produce shared resources and coordinated benefit that neither closure nor brokerage explains alone - this is a design question (rules, monitoring, sanctions), not just a structural one.

Coleman's closure and Burt's brokerage are frequently presented in the literature as being in tension (dense networks that build trust also tend to circulate redundant information), which is a genuinely useful tool for the book: most client organizations have over-invested in one and starved the other, and the Five-Finger Model's "Design" stage should ask which is missing.

## 4. What is the role of trust?

Mayer, Davis & Schoorman (SC-008) supply the field's dominant operational definition: trust is "the willingness of a party to be vulnerable to the actions of another party," predicted by perceived ability, benevolence, and integrity (the ABI model) plus the trustor's own propensity to trust. This is directly useful to Palma as a diagnostic: when a stakeholder relationship is active but low-trust, the ABI model lets Palma isolate whether the deficit is about competence, care, or principle - three different interventions.

Trust functions as the mechanism that converts a network position into usable capital: Coleman's closure argument and Ostrom's design principles both depend on trust to make cooperation self-enforcing rather than requiring external coercion. Edmondson's psychological-safety research (SC-013) supplies a complementary, team-level operationalization - a shared belief that speaking up will not be punished - and evidence that safety (not comfort or conflict-avoidance) predicts learning and performance. The 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer (SC-014) supplies current, executive-relevant evidence that trust is real but fragmenting into insular circles - reinforcing the book's core distinction between reputation (broad awareness) and access (trusted, reciprocal relationship).

## 5. What is the role of weak ties and brokerage?

Granovetter's 1973 finding (SC-004) - that most job changers in his sample found work through contacts they saw only occasionally, not close friends - is the founding empirical demonstration that novel information and opportunity travel disproportionately through weak ties, because strong-tie clusters circulate redundant information. Burt (SC-005) reframes and sharpens the mechanism structurally: what matters is not tie "weakness" per se but whether the tie bridges a structural hole between otherwise unconnected clusters. His 2004 study is the strongest quantified business evidence in this literature map connecting brokerage position to real organizational outcomes (idea quality, pay, promotion).

For the book, this argues against a common failure mode Palma will need to name explicitly: organizations that measure relationship health by the *strength* or *warmth* of existing ties (client loyalty scores, NPS, engagement metrics on an existing base) while remaining blind to whether their network has any bridging ties at all - the source of new opportunity, according to this literature, comes disproportionately from the bridges, not the warmth.

## 6. How do norms and reciprocity emerge?

Gouldner (SC-009) supplies the foundational sociological account: a generalized moral norm of reciprocity - obligations to help those who have helped you and not harm them - functions as a near-universal stabilizing mechanism because it lets people extend value without a contract, on the expectation that the norm itself enforces repayment. This is the most direct academic grounding available for the book's central proposition ("the true unit of communication is not a message, it is a response") - Gouldner's norm is precisely the mechanism by which an initial message becomes an expected, eventually normative exchange.

Coleman's closure findings and Ostrom's design principles (particularly monitoring and graduated sanctions) describe the structural conditions under which reciprocity norms get reliably enforced rather than eroding into free-riding, which is Olson's (SC-011) default prediction absent those conditions. The book should present these three together as a single causal chain: Gouldner explains *why* reciprocity is a normative expectation once established; Olson explains *why it doesn't emerge automatically* in larger or looser groups; Ostrom (and Coleman's closure mechanism) explain *what design features make it stick* once an organization tries to build it deliberately.

## 7. What are the limits and risks?

This question is treated in depth in the companion Anti-Palma Argument Map (`research/anti-palma/anti-palma-argument-map.md`), but the theoretical literature itself already contains the seeds of the critique: Bourdieu's framing makes social capital inherently about stratified advantage, not neutral cooperation - a relationship system designed without attention to who is excluded will tend to reproduce existing access patterns. Coleman's closure mechanism, useful for trust and norm enforcement, is also the literature's classic explanation for groupthink, conformity pressure, and reduced external information flow (in tension with Granovetter/Burt). Olson's free-rider logic is a standing warning that most "communities" will not self-organize without deliberate incentive design - the default outcome of an unstructured audience is passivity, not spontaneous reciprocity.

## Cross-cutting synthesis for the Five-Finger Model

Mapping the literature onto Palma's provisional Listen / Map / Design / Activate / Measure sequence:

**Listen** draws methodologically on ethnographic and interview traditions represented by Wenger (SC-007) and Edmondson (SC-013) - both depend on getting past formal, guarded accounts to what people actually experience.

**Map** draws on Freeman's stakeholder theory (SC-010), Granovetter and Burt's tie/structure distinctions (SC-004, SC-005), and Barabási's hub concept (SC-012) - the question is not just who the stakeholders are, but what kind of ties connect them and where the structural holes and hubs sit.

**Design** draws most heavily on Ostrom's eight principles (SC-006), Wenger's domain/community/practice triad (SC-007), and Olson's selective-incentive logic (SC-011) - this is where the literature is most directly prescriptive and most directly useful for the book's practice chapters.

**Activate** has comparatively thin direct theoretical grounding in this literature map (the sources here are more diagnostic and design-oriented than activation-oriented) - this is a candidate gap; see the missing-theories note for suggested additions (e.g., action research, rapid experimentation literature already listed under Track G).

**Measure** draws on the trust literature (SC-008, SC-014), Edmondson's caution about counterintuitive metrics (more reported errors can indicate more safety, not less), and Barabási's concentration-risk implication (SC-012) - reinforcing Section 6's instruction not to reduce all value to one vanity score.
