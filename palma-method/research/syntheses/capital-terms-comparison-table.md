**Purpose:** Per REVIEW_CLAUDE_0001 (R-004) and TASK_CLAUDE_0004, this table supports the editorial decision on Chapter 6's definition of Relationship Capital. It is a decision-support tool, not an expansion of the literature review, and it does not itself resolve OQ-003 - it lays the four terms side by side so the editorial decision can be made with the actual distinctions visible.

---

| | **Social Capital** | **Relationship Capital** | **Relational Capital** | **Stakeholder Capital** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Origin / field** | Sociology (Bourdieu 1986, Coleman 1988, Putnam 2000) | No single canonical academic origin; closest institutional use is IIRC's combined "Social and Relationship Capital" category (2013); otherwise used loosely in management/marketing writing, including by Palma | Intellectual-capital accounting literature (e.g., Edvinsson & Malone and related 1990s-2000s IC frameworks), where it names one of three IC components alongside human capital and structural capital | Not a standard academic term; closest grounding is Freeman's stakeholder theory (1984), which theorizes *stakeholders* and *relationships* but does not itself coin "stakeholder capital" as a measurable capital stock |
| **Unit of analysis** | Varies by theorist: individual/family (Bourdieu), relationship/structure (Coleman), community/society (Putnam) | Typically the organization or a specific relationship the organization holds | The firm, in accounting/reporting terms - its stock of external relationship value (customers, suppliers, alliances) | The firm's relationship to its full stakeholder set (employees, customers, communities, regulators, shareholders) |
| **What it names** | The general phenomenon: value embedded in social structure and access it enables, independent of any one institution's accounting needs | An applied, diagnostic, buildable asset a company can design and manage - Palma's own operationalization | A reportable, quantifiable-in-principle component of a firm's intangible value, for investor/accounting purposes | The idea that treating multiple stakeholder groups' interests as legitimate, not just shareholders', is itself a source of durable value |
| **Established academic discipline?** | Yes - a mature, decades-old sociological research tradition with three competing canonical definitions | **No.** Not established as its own academic discipline; this must not be claimed in the manuscript (explicit constraint, TASK_CLAUDE_0004) | Yes, within the narrower intellectual-capital accounting literature, but that literature itself is smaller and less canonical than social capital sociology | No - "stakeholder theory" is established (Freeman 1984 onward); "stakeholder capital" as a named, measurable capital category is not standard usage |
| **Closest primary source found in this research** | Bourdieu 1986 (SC-001); Coleman 1988 (SC-002); Putnam 2000 (SC-003) | IIRC Six Capitals Framework, "Social and Relationship Capital" (SC-019) - combined with social capital, not separate | Not directly source-carded in this pass; flagged as a research gap (see below) | Freeman 1984 (SC-010) |
| **Measurability** | Contested; no standardized cross-context metric (see anti-Palma Q8, ROI difficulty) | Palma's task: define measurable proxies (trust scores, participation, referral flow) as a design choice, not an inherited standard | Accounting frameworks attempt monetary/quasi-monetary disclosure, with acknowledged difficulty and inconsistency | Even less standardized than relational capital; mostly qualitative stakeholder-mapping tools (see SC-021-adjacent stakeholder-mapping search results) |
| **Recommended editorial treatment for the book** | Cite as the academic foundation Relationship Capital draws on, with the three competing definitions kept distinct (per literature-map.md) | Present explicitly as Palma's own applied managerial term, grounded in but not equivalent to academic social capital - state this lineage plainly in Chapter 6's opening | Cite as the nearest existing "official" institutional cousin (via IIRC), useful for giving business readers a familiar anchor, but do not conflate it with Relationship Capital as if they were the same term | Cite Freeman as the theoretical grounding for *why* multiple stakeholder groups matter, without inventing a "stakeholder capital" metric that doesn't exist in the literature |

## Editorial recommendation

Relationship Capital should be introduced in Chapter 6 as: *a managerial concept developed by Palma, grounded in established research on social capital (Bourdieu, Coleman, Putnam), trust (Mayer/Davis/Schoorman, Zak), reciprocity (Gouldner), stakeholder theory (Freeman), network science (Granovetter, Burt, Barabási), and communities of practice (Wenger) - and echoed, though not directly named, in the accounting profession's own "Social and Relationship Capital" reporting category (IIRC, since 2013).* This framing satisfies the explicit constraint that the book must not claim Relationship Capital is an established academic discipline, while still giving it credible intellectual lineage and one recognizable institutional cousin.

## Research gap flagged

"Relational capital" in intellectual-capital accounting literature (Edvinsson & Malone and related sources) was referenced in the literature map (OQ-003 discussion) but not directly source-carded in this or the prior research pass. If Chapter 6 wants to cite relational capital directly rather than only via the IIRC's combined category, a targeted follow-up card is recommended before manuscript finalization. This is noted as new Missing Evidence under Chapter 6 in that chapter's packet.
