# Chapter 17 — Diagnose (packet)

**Maps to core phases:** Orient + Diagnose (Palma Method Canon v1.0 §3, §6).

## Purpose

Establish, for the reader, why a communication or relationship problem cannot be responsibly acted on before two separate questions are answered: what is actually being asked for (Orient), and what relationship reality is actually present, as distinct from what is claimed or assumed (Diagnose). The chapter's job is to make the reader distrust the first framing of a problem — their own client's framing, and their own first read of it — without leaving them paralyzed by that distrust.

## Central argument

A request arrives dressed as a solution ("we need a community," "we need better engagement," "we need a video"). Treating that request as a fact rather than a hypothesis is the single most common origin of a wasted engagement. Orient exists to convert the request into something testable. Diagnose exists to test it — against the actual social form present, the smallest unit of analysis that explains the problem, and at least one rival explanation for what's going wrong. Only once both are done does a decision have anything real to stand on.

## Phase mapping

- **Orient:** situation, boundary, mandate, affected people, the real question (DECISION_0005). Operationally: restate the brief as a hypothesis; name the strategic purpose it's meant to serve; state a provisional level of analysis without committing to it; run the first two decision-gate checks (is there a relational dimension at all; is the client's own framing already sufficient).
- **Diagnose:** relationship reality and the gap between stated and observed conditions (DECISION_0005). Operationally: check whether a "community" label is actually earned; assign the smallest sufficient unit of analysis (message, situation, recurring pattern, relationship, role, microgroup, team, organization, network, or public/community/ecosystem); build the Relational and Interaction Map with real relationship content — history, expectation, trust, power — not a connection diagram; name a rival explanation and say why it was set aside.

## Claims / evidence table

| Claim | Evidentiary basis | Status |
|---|---|---|
| A stated brief is a hypothesis, not a diagnosis | Realist evaluation (Pawson & Tilley) — mechanism and context determine outcome, not the presenting request; Weick/CCO — organizations are constituted through talk, not simply described by it | Well-supported, foundational theory |
| Community must be diagnosed, not assumed from a label | Simmel (group size changes social form); Lave & Wenger (participation trajectories are structured, not declared); Fine (organizations are usually an archipelago of microgroups, not one community) | Well-supported |
| The right unit of analysis is usually smaller than the one first assumed | Goffman (the situation as its own analyzable unit); Collins (interaction, not organization, is where the mechanism usually lives) | Well-supported, live research line |
| A named rival explanation improves diagnostic reliability | General methodological principle from realist evaluation and process consultation (Schein) — not a claim specific to Palma, imported as discipline | Well-supported as a general research practice; Palma's specific application is untested at scale |
| First-hand interviewing produces more reliable diagnostic evidence than a client's account of a third party | Interviewing methodology (Fitzpatrick; Kvale & Brinkmann; social-desirability-bias literature) | Well-supported in the interviewing literature; not independently validated for Palma's specific engagements |

## Practical tools

Problem Framing and Social Form Assessment artifacts (Six Artifacts v0.1); the Decision Tree's Nodes A–D (including the multi-audience fork and the escalate/de-escalate loop); the Interviewing Practice Layer's technique map by unit of analysis (Mom Test past-behavior questions, laddering, critical incident technique, elite-interviewing rules, source-directness tracking); the Negotiation Practice Layer's application to the first framing conversation (interests over positions, before the brief hardens).

## Counterarguments

A practitioner under commercial pressure will reasonably object that full diagnosis before any commitment is a luxury few real client relationships allow — scope and price often get fixed before diagnosis can run to completion. This is a legitimate operational constraint, not a flaw in the argument for diagnosing seriously; it is addressed structurally in Chapter 18, not waved away here. A second reasonable objection: insisting on a rival explanation for every diagnostic claim can become its own performance, a box checked rather than a real test — the chapter should name this risk rather than pretend rigor is self-enforcing.

## Risks of overclaiming

This chapter must not imply that following Orient and Diagnose produces a correct diagnosis, only a disciplined one. It must not claim that a named rival explanation was actually, empirically ruled out by any real Palma engagement — no completed case is available to cite, and none should be invented. It must not present the unit-of-analysis ladder as a settled instrument; it is a working heuristic built from established theory, not a validated measurement tool.

## Draft structure (for the manuscript prose)

1. Open on the gap between what a request says and what it needs (no invented scene — stated as a general pattern).
2. Orient: the discipline of treating the brief as a hypothesis.
3. Diagnose: the social-form check, the unit-of-analysis ladder, the mandatory rival explanation.
4. The craft underneath the discipline: how evidence is actually gathered without producing a rehearsed or polite answer.
5. What a genuinely complete Diagnose phase looks like, and what happens when it can't complete cleanly (bridge to Chapter 18's fixed-scope reality).

## Sources

Goffman (1959, 1967, 1974); Collins (2004); Fine (1979); Simmel (1908/2009); Lave & Wenger (1991); Wenger (1998); Weick (1995); Taylor & Van Every (2000); Pawson & Tilley (1997); Schein (1999, 2013); Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test*; Kvale & Brinkmann; Reynolds & Gutman (1988); Flanagan (1954); Aberbach & Rockman (2002) — full citations as verified in Palma Canon v0.1 Part 1 and the Interviewing Practice Layer v0.1.

## Verification gaps

No independent, peer-reviewed validation exists for Palma's specific unit-of-analysis ladder as an instrument (it is original synthesis built on established theory, not itself an established instrument). The claim that Diagnose-phase discipline improves downstream decisions has not been tested against any completed, citable Palma engagement in material available to this task.

## Editorial decisions

Written at the level of principle and mechanism, not through a worked example — no approved case exists for this chapter to draw on, and inventing one is explicitly prohibited by this task's authorization. Where the underlying working documents (Core, Decision Tree, Six Artifacts, Interviewing/Negotiation Practice Layers) contained a retroactive check against the restricted case file, that check and its findings are not carried into this chapter or its packet.
