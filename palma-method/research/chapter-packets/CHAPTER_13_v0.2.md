## 1. Chapter Purpose

Chapter 13 addresses how an organization translates a diagnosed gap between current and required relationships - or an already-existing, previously undesigned social group discovered through mapping or activation - into a deliberate set of conditions: channels, roles, rituals, governance, and value exchange. It is explicitly the design of conditions, not the engineering of community, and its central discipline is distinguishing what an organization can specify from what it cannot.

## 2. Reader Transformation

Before this chapter, a reader is likely to think of "design" as message design. After this chapter, the reader should be able to distinguish message design from system design, distinguish designing a condition from manufacturing an outcome, state explicitly which of five components a given design decision touches, and identify - for any one of those components - what the organization is actually specifying versus what it is asking participants, or reality, to supply.

## 3. Central Argument

Designing for relationships is a categorically different task from designing a campaign. A relationship system needs channels, roles, rituals, governance, and value exchange - and this chapter states directly, per TASK_CLAUDE_0012's instruction that this distinction survive contact with the sociology brief, that organizations can design **conditions** but cannot design **outcomes**. An organization can specify a channel; it cannot specify that trust will flow through it. It can offer a role; it cannot guarantee someone will take it up in the spirit intended. It can schedule a recurring gathering; it cannot manufacture the shared mood and mutual focus (Collins, SC-027) that turns a gathering into a ritual capable of producing durable attachment, and it certainly cannot manufacture the idioculture (Fine, SC-038) a group may or may not generate from repeated contact with each other. This is not a limitation current design technique will eventually overcome - it is a structural fact about the difference between a condition and a social outcome, and every one of this chapter's five components rests on an assumption about how people will actually behave that a rigorous design process makes explicit, in theory-of-change form, precisely so it can be tested and revised rather than discovered to be wrong only after failure. Community design that specifies every interaction in advance, leaving participants no real authorship over the system they are being invited into, has crossed from design into control - a different, and differently risky, activity this chapter treats as a matter of degree along an identifiable direction, not a bright line.

## 4. Key Distinctions

- **Message design vs. system design.** A message is a single communication artifact; a system is the durable structure that determines whether repeated, meaningful participation is even possible.
- **Conditions vs. outcomes (new framing, v0.2).** A condition is something an organization can specify directly - a channel exists, a role is defined, a meeting recurs on a schedule, a rule is published. An outcome is a social result - trust, shared identity, solidarity, a functioning idioculture - that can only emerge from what participants actually do with a condition, not from the condition's specification alone. Every one of this chapter's five components has a condition-layer an organization designs and an outcome-layer it cannot.
- **Invitation vs. participation.** Being invited is not the same as participating.
- **Tactic vs. practice.** A tactic is a discrete action; a practice is a repeated, socially recognized pattern of behavior.
- **Activity vs. mechanism.** An activity is something that happens; a mechanism is the underlying reason it produces, or fails to produce, a given effect.
- **Output vs. outcome.** An output is what the design produces directly; an outcome is the relational change the design was meant to cause.
- **Community design vs. control.** Design shapes conditions under which participants can act; control specifies and constrains what participants are permitted to do so tightly that no real authorship remains.
- **Practice vs. institutionalization** (per the canonical terminology note in `PART_III_ARCHITECTURE_DECISION_v0.1.md`). A practice is a repeated way of doing something, potentially carried by a group, and can exist without ever being institutionalized. Institutionalization is the organizational embedding of expectations, roles, routines, authority, and resources so a practice persists independent of any single person - and institutionalization can also fossilize a practice, or separate it from the purpose that originally animated it. This chapter designs conditions that may produce a practice; whether and how that practice should be institutionalized is addressed further in Chapters 14 and 15, and is not automatically desirable (see the design/control distinction above).

Elinor Ostrom's research on enduring, well-governed commons institutions (SC-006, VI-001) supplies grounding for governance. Randall Collins's ritual-chain research (SC-027) supplies the mechanism for why rituals belong in a system design. Amy Edmondson's psychological safety research (SC-013) is reused as a precondition for participation architecture.

New in v0.2, from `SOCIOLOGY_OF_MICROCOMMUNITIES_RESEARCH_v0.1.md`, used per that brief's own decisions: Gary Alan Fine's idioculture research (SC-038) directly supports this chapter's central conditions/outcomes distinction - local norms and shared references are an emergent output of designed conditions, not something a designer can specify into existence, which strengthens rather than undermines this chapter's existing theory-of-change discipline. Lamont & Molnár's symbolic/social boundary distinction (SC-041) supports this chapter's expanded governance discussion of boundaries: a governance rule can specify a social boundary (who formally has access to what) but cannot specify a symbolic boundary (whether people actually feel like insiders), and confusing the two is a specific design error this chapter now names directly.

## 6. Proposed Architecture

This chapter proposes five components of a relationship-system design, specified together, not as sequential steps, and explicitly not a named six-part method or a substitute for the separate Palma Method architecture:

1. **Channels** - the paths through which participation can occur.
2. **Roles** - differentiated ways of being involved beyond "audience member."
3. **Rituals and rhythms** - the recurring, structured interactions that convert a channel and a role into an actual, repeated practice.
4. **Governance** - explicit rules about who decides what, including explicit boundaries.
5. **Value exchange** - a stated or clearly implicit answer to what participants receive for what they contribute.

**New in v0.2 — the specify/enact/emerge/fail-or-mutate table, directly operationalizing Problem D:**

| Component | What the organization can specify | What participants must enact | What can only emerge | What may fail or mutate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Channels | That a path exists, who has access to it, its format | Whether they actually use it, and for what | Whether the channel becomes a site of focused interaction (Goffman, SC-037) or remains unfocused copresence | Channel goes unused; channel gets used for a purpose other than intended |
| Roles | That a role is defined, its formal responsibilities and privileges | Whether someone takes the role up, and how they interpret it once in it | Whether the role-holder is actually recognized and trusted by others in that role | Role goes unfilled; role is filled but not recognized informally; role holder over- or under-reaches the formal definition |
| Rituals and rhythms | That a gathering recurs on a schedule, its format and stated purpose | Whether participants show up with genuine mutual focus, not just attendance | Whether the gathering meets Collins's structural conditions (co-presence, boundary, mutual focus, shared mood) and produces durable attachment | Ritual becomes rote attendance with no shared mood; ritual produces attachment to the facilitator rather than the group |
| Governance | That rules exist, who formally decides what, a published boundary (social boundary, per Lamont & Molnár) | Whether participants treat the rules as legitimate | Whether a symbolic boundary (felt belonging) forms alongside the specified social boundary | Rules experienced as arbitrary or imposed; formal boundary diverges sharply from felt boundary |
| Value exchange | A stated or implicit offer of what participants receive | Whether participants perceive the exchange as fair, and reciprocate | Whether reciprocity (Gouldner, SC-009) becomes a stable, mutually understood norm | Exchange perceived as extractive; norm of reciprocity never forms despite a stated offer |

No component's "what can only emerge" column is something a redesign can force into existence by better specification. It can only be conditioned for, observed, and responded to - which is precisely Chapter 14's task, not this chapter's.

## 13. Draft Chapter

A campaign has a start date, an end date, and a message. A relationship system has none of these by design - it is meant to persist, to be used differently by different participants, and to produce outcomes no single message could reliably cause on its own. This chapter is about translating a diagnosed gap, or an already-existing social group an organization did not itself design, into an actual operating system of conditions.

That system needs several distinct components, and no single one of them substitutes for the others. **Channels** are the paths through which participation can occur. **Roles** are differentiated ways of being involved. **Rituals and rhythms** are the recurring, structured interactions that convert a channel and a role into a lived practice. **Governance** specifies who decides what, including explicit boundaries. **Value exchange** states what participants receive for what they give.

The discipline this chapter now states more directly than its earlier draft did: every one of these five components has two layers, and confusing them is the single most common design error this chapter exists to prevent. There is a layer an organization can actually specify - that a channel exists, that a role is formally defined, that a gathering is scheduled, that a rule is published, that an exchange is offered. And there is a layer that only emerges from what participants actually do with that specification - whether the channel becomes a site of real, focused exchange or sits technically open and functionally unused; whether someone takes up a role in the spirit it was designed for; whether a scheduled gathering actually produces the shared mood and mutual focus Randall Collins's research identifies as necessary for durable attachment, or remains an attendance obligation nobody feels anything about; whether a governance rule is experienced as legitimate; whether an offered exchange is perceived as fair and reciprocated. Gary Alan Fine's research on idioculture makes this distinction impossible to soften: the shared knowledge, references, and norms a genuine microgroup develops through recurring interaction are an emergent property of that interaction, not something a designer specifies into existence by writing a good channel description or a well-worded role definition. An organization can design the conditions under which idioculture becomes more likely to form. It cannot design the idioculture itself, any more than it can design trust, identity, or solidarity directly - a distinction the sociology of microcommunities makes plain and this chapter now states as a structural fact about the task, not a limitation of current design technique.

No case has been inserted here, consistent with the instruction not to invent or infer details standing in for a real example.]*

None of the five components can be correctly specified once and then executed mechanically. Carol Weiss's foundational work on theory-based evaluation supplies this chapter's central discipline for handling that uncertainty honestly: state the assumption explicitly, in if-then form, before implementation, and pair every stated assumption with an explicit failure condition.

Governance deserves particular care, and this chapter extends its treatment in v0.2 to address boundaries directly, using vocabulary the sociology brief supplies. A governance decision can specify a **social boundary** - who formally has access to a channel, a role, a decision process - in exactly the sense the conditions column of this chapter's new table describes. What it cannot specify is whether a corresponding **symbolic boundary** forms - whether the people inside that formal boundary actually feel like a distinct "us," and whether the people outside it perceive the boundary as real and meaningful. Michèle Lamont and Virág Molnár's research on social boundaries shows these two are related but not identical: an organization can formally grant a group access (specifying a social boundary favorably) while that group never develops any felt sense of belonging, and conversely a strong felt sense of belonging can exist among people with no formal boundary drawn around them at all. Elinor Ostrom's research on long-enduring, well-governed commons institutions remains this chapter's clearest account of what governance design needs to include if it is to avoid becoming disguised control: participants having a genuine, structural voice in setting the rules that govern them, monitoring and enforcement seen as legitimate, and recognized rights to organize and revise those rules on their own terms.

This is the line this chapter draws between design and control. Design shapes the conditions under which participants can act, leaving genuine room to use those conditions in ways the designer did not fully script in advance. Control specifies and constrains so completely that no real authorship remains. The direction of a given design choice, once named plainly, is usually identifiable: does this rule exist to make genuine participation safer and more likely, or does it exist to make participation more predictable and controllable for the organization's convenience.

No organization designs a single relationship in isolation, and this chapter's five components should be specified with the whole relationship portfolio in view. Designing with the portfolio in view also means confronting resource constraints honestly: partial, explicit design is preferable to an elaborate design specified only on paper for a portfolio the organization cannot actually resource.

This is also where the output/outcome distinction needs to be stated directly in design terms. A design's outputs are what it visibly produces. Its outcomes are the relational changes it was actually built to cause. A design can generate strong outputs and weak outcomes simultaneously, and a design process that only tracks whether its components are being used, without asking whether they are producing the relational change they were built for, will not notice this gap until Chapter 15's measurement work forces the question.

Communication functions inside this system as a service and a behavior, not a layer applied on top of it afterward.

A final component belongs inside the design itself: feedback mechanisms. A relationship-system design that does not build a feedback mechanism into its own channels, roles, and rituals is repeating, at the design level, the exact gap Chapter 11 warned against.

One closing distinction, made necessary by this chapter's expanded scope in v0.2: this chapter's five-component architecture is a set of considerations to specify together, drawn from and consistent with concepts this book has already established (rituals, reciprocity, governance, access), not a new named methodology. It should not be read, and this book does not intend it to be read, as a complete consulting framework that anticipates, competes with, or substitutes for the separate architecture the organization's own Palma Method work is developing elsewhere. Where the two might appear to overlap - both, for instance, treat channels and rituals as meaningful design categories - this chapter's five components remain scoped specifically to what this book's argument about Relationship Capital requires, not to a standalone operating system an organization could implement independent of the rest of this book.
