## 1. Chapter Purpose

Chapter 13 answers what an organization does with the visibility Chapter 12's mapping produced: translate a diagnosed gap between current and required relationships into a deliberate operating system - channels, roles, rituals, governance, and value exchange - rather than a single campaign or a well-produced event (the failure mode Chapter 5 already documented in detail).

## 2. Reader Transformation

Before this chapter, a reader is likely to think of "design" as message design - crafting the right content, the right invitation, the right campaign. After this chapter, the reader should be able to distinguish message design from system design, recognize that an invitation is not participation and a well-attended activity is not a durable practice, and be able to state, in explicit theory-of-change terms, what assumptions a given design rests on and what conditions would indicate the design has failed.

## 3. Central Argument

Designing for relationships is a categorically different task from designing a campaign, because a campaign is built to produce a response to a message, while a relationship system is built to produce and sustain ongoing participation - and the two require different components entirely. A relationship system needs channels (paths through which participation can occur), roles (differentiated ways of being involved, not just "audience"), rituals (per Chapter 5's already-established distinction between an event and a ritual chain), governance (explicit rules about who decides what), and value exchange (a stated or implicit answer to what participants receive in return for what they give). None of these components can be fully specified in advance and then executed mechanically - every design rests on stated or unstated assumptions about how people will actually behave, and a rigorous design process makes those assumptions explicit, in theory-of-change form, precisely so they can be tested and revised rather than discovered to be wrong only after failure. Communication, in this model, functions as a service and a behavior embedded in the system, not a broadcast layered on top of it - and community design that specifies every interaction in advance, leaving participants no real authorship over the system they are being invited into, has crossed from design into control, which is a different, and differently risky, activity.

## 4. Key Distinctions

- **Message design vs. system design.** A message is a single communication artifact; a system is the durable structure (channels, roles, rituals, governance, exchange) that determines whether repeated, meaningful participation is even possible.
- **Invitation vs. participation.** Being invited is not the same as participating; per Chapter 11's Arnstein-derived distinction, an invitation with no real influence attached is closer to informing than to participation.
- **Tactic vs. practice.** A tactic is a discrete action (a post, an event, an email); a practice is a repeated, socially recognized pattern of behavior that participants come to expect and enact themselves, per Chapter 5's ritual-chain logic.
- **Activity vs. mechanism.** An activity is something that happens; a mechanism is the underlying reason it produces (or fails to produce) a given effect - a design built around activities without understanding the mechanism they depend on will not transfer to a new context reliably.
- **Output vs. outcome.** An output is what the design produces directly (attendance, posts, sign-ups); an outcome is the relational change the design was meant to cause (trust, reciprocity, access, relationship strength, network structure) - outputs are easier to observe and are routinely mistaken for outcomes, a distinction developed further in Chapter 15.
- **Community design vs. control.** Design shapes conditions under which participants can act; control specifies and constrains what participants are permitted to do so tightly that no real authorship remains - the line between the two is a matter of degree, not a bright rule, but the direction of a given design choice is usually identifiable.

## 5. Research Synthesis

Carol Weiss's theory-of-change methodology (SC-035) supplies this chapter's central discipline: a relationship-system design is, whether stated or not, a chain of assumptions about how specific components (a ritual, a role, a governance rule) will produce a specific relational change, and making that chain explicit is what allows a design to be evaluated and revised rather than simply judged a success or failure in hindsight. Elinor Ostrom's research on enduring, well-governed commons institutions (SC-006, carrying VI-001's page-verification caveat) supplies this chapter's grounding for governance and rules-and-boundaries: the long-enduring systems Ostrom studied paired closure and mutual obligation with participants having real voice in setting the rules that governed them, monitoring that was visible and legitimate, and recognized rights to self-organize - a substantive, not merely procedural, account of what governance design needs to include. Randall Collins's ritual-chain research (SC-027), already established in Chapter 5, supplies the mechanism for why rituals belong in a system design rather than being treated as a nice-to-have: a designed ritual that fails to meet Collins's four conditions (co-presence, boundary, mutual focus, shared mood) will not produce durable attachment regardless of how well-produced it is. Alvin Gouldner's reciprocity norm (SC-009) supplies the theoretical grounding for value exchange as a design component, not an afterthought. Amy Edmondson's psychological safety research (SC-013) is reused here as a precondition for participation architecture: a channel or role that requires interpersonal risk-taking will only be used at the level of psychological safety participants actually feel, not the level a design document assumes.

## 6. Proposed Architecture

This chapter proposes five components of a relationship-system design, presented explicitly as components to be specified together, not sequential steps:

1. **Channels** - the paths through which participation can occur (who can reach whom, and how, building on Chapter 12's mapped current-vs-required relationships).
2. **Roles** - differentiated ways of being involved beyond "audience member" (host, contributor, mentor, connector), each with a different relationship to the system's value exchange.
3. **Rituals and rhythms** - the recurring, structured interactions (per Chapter 5) that convert a channel and a role into an actual, repeated practice.
4. **Governance** - explicit rules about who decides what, including rules about the rules themselves (per Ostrom's design principles), and explicit boundaries (who is in, who is out, and on what basis).
5. **Value exchange** - a stated or clearly implicit answer to what participants receive for what they contribute, consistent with Gouldner's reciprocity norm and this book's earlier caution (Chapter 4) against disguising unpaid extraction as belonging.

Each component's design should be accompanied by an explicit, stated theory of change (what assumption connects this component to the intended relational outcome) and an explicit failure condition (what observation would indicate the assumption was wrong) - both carried forward into Chapter 15's measurement practice.

## 9. Counterarguments

1. A five-component design framework may be more structure than small organizations or limited engagements can realistically implement. *(Addressed in the draft: components can be specified minimally - a single stated rule counts as a governance component - the requirement is explicitness, not elaborateness.)*
2. Making assumptions explicit in theory-of-change form can create false confidence or rigidity, causing designers to defend an articulated theory rather than revise it when early evidence contradicts it. *(Addressed by this chapter's explicit pairing of every assumption with a stated failure condition, intended to make revision easier, not harder - carried into Chapter 14's adaptive posture.)*
3. Treating community design and control as points on a single spectrum may understate cases where firm rules are themselves what participants want and value (e.g., safety-critical or reputation-sensitive communities). *(Addressed by this chapter's framing of the design/control distinction as a matter of degree and direction, not a claim that all constraint is illegitimate.)*

## 14. Draft Chapter

A campaign has a start date, an end date, and a message. A relationship system has none of these by design - it is meant to persist, to be used differently by different participants, and to produce outcomes no single message could reliably cause on its own. Treating relationship-system design as a more ambitious version of campaign design is a category error with predictable consequences: an organization ends up with a well-crafted invitation and nothing durable for anyone to actually join once they accept it. This chapter is about the second task, not the first - translating a diagnosed gap between an organization's current and required relationships (Chapter 12's output) into an actual operating system.

That system needs several distinct components, and no single one of them, done well, substitutes for the others. **Channels** are the paths through which participation can occur - not just broadcast channels, but paths that allow movement in both directions, building directly on the current-versus-required relationship gaps Chapter 12's mapping identifies. **Roles** are differentiated ways of being involved: host, contributor, mentor, connector, newcomer - each carrying a different relationship to the system's rhythms and a different implicit value exchange, and each a deliberate alternative to the single undifferentiated category of "audience" this book has been arguing against since its opening chapters. **Rituals and rhythms** are the recurring, structured interactions that, per Chapter 5's already-established argument, are what actually convert a channel and a role into a lived practice rather than a standing invitation nobody uses - a ritual that fails to meet Collins's four structural conditions (co-presence, a boundary, mutual focus, shared mood) will not produce durable attachment no matter how appealing the underlying idea is. **Governance** specifies who decides what, including rules about how the rules themselves can change, and explicit boundaries about who is in and who is out and on what basis. And **value exchange** states, or makes clearly evident without stating, what participants receive for what they give - a component this book's Chapter 4 already flagged as commonly disguised as pure belonging when it is, in practice, unpaid extraction.

None of these five components can be correctly specified once and then executed mechanically, and this chapter treats that as a structural fact about the task, not a limitation of current knowledge that better design technique could eventually eliminate. Every one of them rests on an assumption about how people will actually behave - that a given channel will be used, that a given role will be taken up, that a given ritual will meet its structural conditions in practice and not just on paper, that a given governance rule will be experienced as legitimate rather than arbitrary, that a given value exchange will be seen by participants as fair. Carol Weiss's foundational work on theory-based evaluation supplies this chapter's central discipline for handling that uncertainty honestly: rather than treating a design as simply right or wrong in hindsight, state the assumption explicitly, in if-then form, before implementation - if we create this role and this rhythm, then this kind of participation should follow, because of this specific mechanism - and pair every stated assumption with an explicit failure condition: what would we observe if this assumption turned out to be wrong. This does not make a design more likely to succeed on the first attempt. It makes a design's actual failures legible and correctable rather than mysterious, which is precisely the posture Chapter 14's adaptive activation work depends on.

Governance deserves particular care, because it is where this chapter's central caution - that relationships cannot be engineered mechanically - is easiest to violate without noticing. Elinor Ostrom's research on long-enduring, well-governed commons institutions offers the clearest available account of what governance design needs to include if it is to avoid becoming, in practice, disguised control. The systems Ostrom studied were not the ones that avoided strong mutual obligation or closure; they were the ones that paired those same ingredients with participants having a genuine, structural voice in setting the rules that governed them, monitoring and enforcement that were visible and broadly seen as legitimate rather than arbitrary, and recognized rights to organize and revise those rules on their own terms rather than having them imposed entirely from outside. This is a substantive standard, not a procedural one: a governance design that solicits comment on rules it has no intention of changing is doing Chapter 11's consultation-not-participation error at the system-design level, not just the individual-decision level, and it carries the same risk of manufacturing cynicism rather than legitimacy.

This is the line this chapter draws between design and control, and it is worth stating plainly rather than leaving implicit. Design shapes the conditions under which participants can act - it builds channels, offers roles, establishes rhythms, and sets boundaries - while leaving participants genuine room to use those conditions in ways the designer did not fully script in advance. Control specifies and constrains what participants are permitted to do so completely that no real authorship remains; the system, however elaborate, becomes a more sophisticated broadcast mechanism wearing the vocabulary of community and participation. The difference is a matter of degree rather than a bright, universally applicable rule, and reasonable designs will sit at different points along it depending on context - a safety-critical professional community may legitimately need firmer rules than a casual interest group. But the direction of any specific design choice, once named plainly, is usually identifiable: does this rule exist to make genuine participation safer and more likely, or does it exist to make participation more predictable and controllable for the organization's convenience. A design process that never asks this question of its own governance choices is at real risk of building the second thing while believing, sincerely, that it built the first.

No organization designs a single relationship in isolation, and this chapter's five components should be specified with the whole relationship portfolio in view, not one stakeholder group at a time. A channel, role, or ritual designed for one part of an organization's relationship portfolio has consequences for the rest of it - a governance rule that grants one group significant voice while leaving another entirely outside the process reproduces, at the design stage, exactly the exclusion and concentration risks Chapter 10 already documented as a predictable cost of unmanaged relationship-building. Designing with the portfolio in view also means confronting resource constraints honestly rather than after the fact: an organization rarely has the capacity to build all five components to full maturity for every stakeholder group at once, and this chapter's position is that partial, explicit design - a single stated governance rule, one differentiated role beyond "audience," one recurring rhythm meeting Collins's structural conditions - is preferable to an elaborate design specified only on paper for a portfolio the organization cannot actually resource. Explicitness about what was and was not designed, and why, is itself a form of honesty this chapter treats as more valuable than false completeness.

This is also where the output/outcome distinction, introduced above as a key distinction, needs to be stated directly in design terms rather than left for the measurement chapter alone to resolve. A design's outputs are what it visibly produces - a channel gets used some number of times, a ritual gets attended, a governance document gets published. Its outcomes are the relational changes it was actually built to cause - measurable, over time, in trust, reciprocity, access, relationship strength, and network structure. A design can generate strong outputs and weak outcomes simultaneously - a well-attended ritual that never builds real reciprocity, a heavily used channel that circulates information without building any trust behind it - and a design process that only tracks whether its components are being used, without asking whether they are producing the relational change they were built for, will not notice this gap until Chapter 15's measurement work forces the question. Naming this distinction at the design stage, not only at the measurement stage, is what allows a designer to ask the right question from the outset: not "will people show up," but "will showing up build the specific thing we designed this component to build."

Communication functions inside this system as a service and a behavior, not a layer applied on top of it afterward. The pathway from attention to participation - someone noticing the system exists, engaging with it once, and eventually taking up one of its differentiated roles - is not produced by a single well-crafted message any more than a relationship, per this book's earlier chapters, is produced by a single well-produced event. It is produced by the system actually working as designed, repeatedly, under real conditions - which is precisely what the next chapter is about.

A final component belongs inside the design itself rather than treated as separate from it: feedback mechanisms. Chapter 11 established listening as an organizational capability that has to be built deliberately; a relationship-system design that does not build a feedback mechanism into its own channels, roles, and rituals is repeating, at the design level, the exact gap that chapter warned against - creating a system capable of producing participation but not capable of learning from it. A feedback mechanism built into the design is different from a feedback mechanism bolted onto it afterward: the first asks, as part of specifying a channel or ritual, how the organization will know whether this component is working as intended and for whom; the second asks that question only once something has already gone visibly wrong. Designing the feedback mechanism alongside the component it monitors, rather than after, is what keeps a design revisable rather than merely repeatable - and revisability, not initial correctness, is the realistic standard this chapter holds every design to, given how much of what a design assumes about human behavior cannot be fully known until real people encounter it under real conditions.
