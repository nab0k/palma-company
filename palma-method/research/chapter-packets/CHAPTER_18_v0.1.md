# Chapter 18 — Design (packet)

**Maps to core phases:** Decide + Design (Palma Method Canon v1.0 §3, §6).

## Purpose

Show how a completed diagnosis becomes a bounded decision, and how that decision becomes an actual set of designed conditions — without either step collapsing into a promise about outcomes the method cannot honestly make. This chapter also has to do the hardest structural work in the book: explain what happens when commercial reality has already fixed a scope before Chapter 17's diagnosis finished, because that is the ordinary case, not the exception.

## Central argument

Decide is not "pick a recommendation" — it is naming a mechanism, a zone (core execution, advisory-only, or out of scope), and an owner, for each recommendation, before anything is built. Design is not "write the deliverable" — it is arranging conditions (access, roles, channels, rituals, governance, value exchange) and stating in advance what evidence would show the arrangement was wrong. The two phases share one discipline: design a condition, never promise the outcome the condition might produce. A message, a ritual, or a governance structure can be well-designed and still fail to produce trust or belonging — that gap is not a defect in the method, it is the method being honest about what design can and cannot deliver.

## Phase mapping

- **Decide:** choose the relationship outcome actually required, including when community is not the goal (DECISION_0005). Operationally: state the mechanism and rival explanation already established in Diagnose; run the ownership and zone-routing checks; apply the object-selection rule (strategic purpose, observed problem, sustaining mechanism, smallest responsibly-changeable object, expected proximal outcome, owner, evidence/review point) to every recommendation; check the ethics screen against the proposal.
- **Design:** conditions, roles, channels, rituals, governance, value exchange, testable assumptions; never claim to manufacture social outcomes (DECISION_0005). Operationally: build the sequenced, owned Action Architecture; state the falsification condition before finalizing; re-run the ethics screen against the concrete design, not just the general idea; check any communication artifact for positioning, memorability, and disclosure before treating it as final.

## Claims / evidence table

| Claim | Evidentiary basis | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Interests, not stated positions, should govern what gets decided | Fisher, Ury & Patton, *Getting to Yes* (Harvard Negotiation Project) | Well-supported, widely adopted negotiation theory |
| A negotiated commercial scope commonly precedes completed diagnosis | Structural feature of consulting and agency engagement models generally (not sourced to a single citation; a working observation about how service engagements are commercially initiated) | Plausible and widely recognized in practice; not independently measured for Palma specifically |
| Designed conditions can be evaluated for likely mechanism without guaranteeing the outcome they aim at | Ostrom (design principles for durable institutions describe conditions, not guaranteed cooperation); Pawson & Tilley (context + mechanism → outcome, contingently) | Well-supported, foundational |
| A message amplifying an undiagnosed or unearned claim is an ethics failure, not a craft failure | Cialdini (persuasion principles are levers on belief, not validators of truth); Core §12 ethics screen (internal Palma doctrine) | Persuasion-principle evidence is well-supported; the ethics-screen application is Palma's own synthesis, not independently validated |
| Naming a falsification condition before finalizing a design improves the design's later evaluability | General principle from realist evaluation and scientific method; not Palma-specific evidence | Well-supported as general epistemic practice |

## Practical tools

Intervention Logic and Action Architecture artifacts (Six Artifacts v0.1), including the persuasion-principle disclosure and the stated-goal-tension log; Decision Tree Nodes E–K (mechanism/rival-explanation check, influence check, zone routing, ownership check, minimum-form check, falsification statement, Stop Rule screen); the diagnosis-inside-fixed-scope variant (Protocol v0.1) for when Decide has to run against a scope already set; the Negotiation Practice Layer (principled negotiation, tactical empathy, anchoring) for the Decide-phase conversation itself; the Message & Positioning Practice Layer (positioning by audience, the SUCCESs memorability check, persuasion-principle ethics line, B2B trust-signal layering) for Design-phase communication artifacts.

## Counterarguments

A commercially-minded reader will object that naming two lists — work deliverable inside the fixed scope, and findings that exceed it — sounds like a way to bill for more work later. The honest answer is that the alternative (silently dropping findings that don't fit the paid scope) is a worse failure, not a more efficient one: a finding that never reaches a decision-maker is functionally the same as no finding at all. A second objection: insisting on a falsification condition before finalizing a design can read as academic caution dressed up as rigor. The chapter should concede that the discipline is imported from research methodology, not native to consulting practice, and argue for it on its own terms rather than assuming the reader already agrees it matters.

## Risks of overclaiming

This chapter must not suggest that a well-designed condition reliably produces the social outcome it targets — Ostrom's own design principles describe durable institutions, not guaranteed ones, and Palma's ethical boundary (design conditions, never promise outcomes) exists precisely because that gap is real, not rhetorical. It must not present the "two lists" fixed-scope variant as a solved problem; it is a procedural mitigation for a structural tension, not a resolution of it. No specific persuasion technique should be presented as reliably ethical in application — the chapter should keep the disclosure requirement (name the real diagnosed fact a technique amplifies) as the actual safeguard, not the technique's reputation.

## Connections

## Draft structure (for the manuscript prose)

1. Open on the asymmetry: diagnosis takes time, commercial agreement often can't wait for it.
2. Decide as a discipline of naming — mechanism, zone, owner — not a discipline of choosing the most appealing option.
3. The two-lists response to a fixed scope, and why silently dropping the second list is the actual risk.
4. Design as arranging conditions, illustrated through the boundary itself (what counts as a condition, what counts as an outcome) rather than a worked example.
5. Where craft (message, negotiation, facilitation) sits underneath the boundary without being licensed to erode it.

## Sources

## Verification gaps

The "two lists" mitigation for fixed-scope diagnosis has not been tested across more than the one restricted case this working track built it from; its generalizability to other engagement shapes is unverified. No B2B trust-signaling claim in the Message Practice Layer has been independently re-verified beyond the single 2024–2025 industry report cited there.
