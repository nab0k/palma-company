## A. Chapter Brief

**Central question:** What is trust, specifically, and how is it actually built, broken, and rebuilt - not as a feeling, but as a mechanism?

**Reader problem:** "Build trust" is one of the most common and least actionable pieces of advice in management writing - it names a goal without giving a leader any way to diagnose what's currently missing or design toward it deliberately.

**Working claim:** Trust breaks down into distinct, diagnosable components - competence, care, and integrity - and each one requires a different fix; treating trust as one undifferentiated quality is itself a common reason trust-building efforts fail.

**Chapter argument:** Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman's ability-benevolence-integrity (ABI) model gives Palma a precise diagnostic tool. The chapter pairs this with Gouldner's reciprocity norm (trust as an expectation built through repeated exchange), Zak's now-verified productivity findings (trust as a measurable business lever, not just a cultural nicety), and Google's own internal Project Aristotle research (trust/psychological safety as the single strongest predictor of team effectiveness among the factors Google measured) - building toward a practical claim: trust is not a mood, it is infrastructure, and it can be built, broken, and repaired through identifiable, different mechanisms depending on which component is actually damaged.

**Expected reader takeaway:** The next time trust is missing in a relationship that matters, ask which of the three - ability, benevolence, or integrity - is actually in question, because "we need to rebuild trust" is not a plan; "we need to demonstrate competence again" or "we need to prove we acted in their interest" are.

## C. Draft v0.1

"We need to rebuild trust" is one of the most common sentences in corporate crisis response, and one of the least useful, because it names a destination without naming a mechanism. Trust is not a single dial that goes up or down. It is made of parts, and which part is broken determines what actually has to happen next.

The clearest operational account of what trust actually is comes from organizational researchers Roger Mayer, James Davis, and F. David Schoorman, whose 1995 model has become the field's dominant working definition: trust is "the willingness of a party to be vulnerable to the actions of another party" - and that willingness is predicted by three distinct perceptions the trusting party holds about the other: **ability** (does this person or organization have the competence to do what they say), **benevolence** (do they actually care about my interests, not just their own), and **integrity** (do they operate by principles I find acceptable, and do they act consistently with what they say they believe). A relationship can score high on one of these and low on another - a competitor might be extremely capable (high ability) and still not be trusted, because nobody believes it's acting in your interest (low benevolence). A well-meaning but chronically unreliable partner might score high on benevolence and low on ability. Naming which leg is actually weak turns "we have a trust problem" from a mood into a diagnosis.

This distinction earns its keep the moment you look at how real trust breaches actually get repaired - or don't. Volkswagen's 2015 emissions scandal was, in ABI terms, primarily an integrity breach: the company had installed software specifically designed to deceive regulators and customers about its cars' emissions. That is not a competence failure or a caring failure in the customer's eyes - it is a direct violation of the third leg, and integrity violations are, by most accounts, the hardest of the three to repair, because they call into question whether anything the company says can be taken at face value going forward. Volkswagen's recovery took years and required sustained, structural change - compensation programs, years of rebuilt relationships with dealers and employees - not because the company lacked competence or warmth, but because it had to re-earn belief in its own honesty from a standing start.

Compare that to CrowdStrike's 2024 global IT outage, caused by a defective software update that disrupted airlines, hospitals, and banks worldwide. This was, in ABI terms, primarily an ability breach, not an integrity one - nobody believed CrowdStrike had deliberately caused the outage; the question was whether the company was actually capable of preventing it from happening again. CEO George Kurtz's response - rapid, specific, non-defensive public explanation of what happened, why, and what would change - addressed the ability question directly and quickly, and the company's trust recovery, while still costly, moved on a substantially faster timeline than Volkswagen's. The same underlying model - ability, benevolence, integrity - explains both the different nature of the two breaches and the different repair timelines, which is exactly what makes it more useful to a leader than a general instruction to "communicate transparently."

Underneath the ABI model sits a second mechanism worth naming explicitly: trust isn't just a perception, it's an expectation built through repeated exchange. Sociologist Alvin Gouldner's account of the reciprocity norm - the near-universal expectation that help given creates an obligation to help back - explains why trust tends to compound or erode gradually rather than resetting instantly after any single interaction. A single transparent statement after a crisis doesn't fully repair integrity; a track record of consistent follow-through, repeated over time, is what actually re-establishes the norm that this party can be relied on to reciprocate honestly. This is the same mechanism the Introduction named as the "missing response" - trust is what accumulates when responses, not just messages, are exchanged reliably over time.

At the team level, this same logic has been tested directly and at scale, by an organization with every incentive to measure it rigorously rather than assume it: Google. Google's People Analytics team studied 180 of its own teams - 115 engineering, 65 sales - using more than 35 statistical models, trying to identify what actually predicted team effectiveness. The answer, ranked in order of importance, was psychological safety first, ahead of dependability, structure and clarity, meaning, and impact - and notably, ahead of factors many leaders assume matter more, like individual talent, seniority, or team size, none of which showed a significant effect in Google's own data. Psychological safety, in the sense Harvard researcher Amy Edmondson originally defined it and Google's study explicitly credits, is essentially trust operating at the team level: a shared belief that no one on the team will be punished or embarrassed for admitting a mistake, asking a question, or raising a concern. It is worth being precise that this is Google's own internal research, not an independently replicated academic study - but it is a rare case of a major organization studying its own trust dynamics with real statistical rigor and publishing what it found, and the direction of the finding (trust/safety beats talent and structure) is hard to dismiss.

The productivity case for taking all of this seriously, rather than treating trust as a values statement, is now directly quantifiable. Research published by Paul Zak in the *Harvard Business Review* found that employees at high-trust companies report 50% higher productivity, 76% more engagement, and 40% less burnout than employees at low-trust companies, alongside less stress, more energy, and fewer sick days - self-reported and correlational figures, not a controlled experiment, but a consistent enough pattern across a large comparison to take seriously as a business case, not just a cultural one. And the 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer adds a current, macro-level data point that closes the loop back to the book's opening argument: people increasingly place their trust not in institutions broadly but in specific, close relationships - their own employer most of all (78% trust, ahead of business, government, and NGOs) - which means the leaders and organizations able to build and maintain trust at the relationship level, using a model precise enough to actually diagnose what's missing, are working with the grain of where trust is actually going, not against it.

There is a status dimension worth naming here too, because trust and status are often conflated and they operate through different, though related, mechanisms. A person or organization can hold high status - visible recognition, prestige, seniority - without being trusted in the ABI sense at all; plenty of well-known, high-status leaders are trusted for their competence and distrusted on integrity or benevolence, or vice versa. Status tends to be granted based on position and track record; trust, per the ABI model, has to be re-earned continuously through demonstrated ability, benevolence, and integrity in each specific relationship. Treating status as a substitute for trust is a common and costly leadership error - it explains why a senior, well-regarded executive can still struggle to get a team to speak up honestly, because status alone does not answer the psychological-safety question Google's research identifies as the strongest predictor of team performance. Status can open a door. It cannot, by itself, make anyone willing to be vulnerable on the other side of it.

Reciprocity is what ties status and trust together over time into something durable rather than situational. Gouldner's norm doesn't just explain why an unanswered message erodes trust - it explains why trust, once established through a track record of genuine exchange, tends to become self-reinforcing rather than something that has to be rebuilt from scratch in every interaction. Each instance of promised value actually delivered adds to a standing balance; each broken promise draws it down, disproportionately, because violations of an established norm carry more weight than a first-time absence of one. This is precisely why the Volkswagen and CrowdStrike comparison earlier in this chapter is instructive rather than incidental: both companies were drawing on an existing reciprocity balance built over years of prior interactions with customers, employees, and partners, and the size of that prior balance - not just the skill of the crisis response itself - shaped how much runway each company had to work with while rebuilding.
