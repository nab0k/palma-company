## 1. Chapter Purpose

Chapter 12 treats mapping as the act of making relationships, influence, access, and dependency visible enough to inform design decisions, while treating every map produced as a provisional, incomplete representation rather than a finished diagnosis. It is not a mandatory second stage: an organization may map because it is starting fresh after listening, because a failed activation (Chapter 14) revealed a structure it did not understand, or because a measurement result (Chapter 15) showed a pattern worth investigating structurally.

## 2. Reader Transformation

Before this chapter, a reader is likely to think of "mapping stakeholders" as listing names by importance, or producing a network diagram that looks authoritative because it is visually complex. After this chapter, the reader should be able to distinguish stakeholder importance from network position, formal authority from informal influence, a symbolic boundary from a social boundary, and a map from a diagnosis - and should treat any specific map as a snapshot built on incomplete data and a specific standpoint, not a finished, self-explaining answer.

## 3. Central Argument

Making relationships, influence, access, and dependency visible requires more than one kind of map, because these are not one property but several, and they do not move together. Stakeholder importance is a different question from network position. Formal authority is a different question from informal influence. Every map an organization produces rests on incomplete data and is built from a specific standpoint - the questions asked, the people interviewed, the relationships that were disclosed - and should be treated as a provisional, time-bound representation rather than a settled diagnosis. A map does not itself explain meaning, trust, or obligation; it shows structure, and structure requires interpretation before it means anything. And a map that captures a group at a single point in time risks reifying something that was never meant to be stable - treating a transient, "wispy" gathering as though it were a durable structure simply because it was visible enough, at the moment of mapping, to be drawn. **(v0.3: this specific caution is a Palma extension of Fine & van den Scott's [SC-039] leisure/fan-culture typology, not a claim the article makes about organizational mapping.)**

## 4. Key Distinctions

- **Stakeholder importance vs. network position.** Importance is a judgment about whose interests matter to a decision; position is a structural fact about where someone sits in the flow of information, trust, access, and support.
- **Formal authority vs. informal influence.** The org chart describes reporting lines; it does not reliably describe who is actually consulted, trusted, or relied upon.
- **Relational access vs. structural access.** Relational access is whether a channel exists to one specific party; structural access is whether a bridge exists into an entire otherwise-unreached part of the network.
- **Relationship strength vs. contact frequency.** Frequent, shallow contact is not the same as a strong relationship.
- **Symbolic boundary vs. social boundary.** New in v0.2, per Lamont & Molnár (SC-041): a symbolic boundary is a perceived insider/outsider distinction people carry in their heads; a social boundary is an objectified pattern of unequal access to resources or opportunity. A map can show a symbolic boundary (people describe themselves as a distinct group) without any accompanying social boundary (no actual difference in access or opportunity follows from it), or the reverse - an unequal access pattern with no group self-identification attached to it at all. Mapping one without the other produces a specific, common error: treating a felt in-group/out-group line as proof of exclusion, or treating a real access disparity as harmless because no one names it as a boundary.
- **Map vs. diagnosis.** A map shows what the data captured; a diagnosis is an interpretation of what that structure means and what, if anything, should change about it.

## 5. Research Synthesis

Freeman's stakeholder theory (SC-010) supplies this chapter's starting distinction. Krackhardt and Hanson's practitioner mapping method (SC-031) - separately diagramming advice, trust, and communication networks - is this chapter's central applied technique. Burt's structural-holes research (SC-005) and Granovetter's weak-ties finding (SC-004) supply the vocabulary for bridges and brokerage positions; Coleman's closure research (SC-002) supplies the vocabulary for clusters. Barabási-derived hub and resilience findings (SC-012, SC-028) are used only as an illustrative lens (VI-002/VI-004 governed).

New in v0.2, from `SOCIOLOGY_OF_MICROCOMMUNITIES_RESEARCH_v0.1.md`: Lamont & Molnár's symbolic/social boundary distinction (SC-041) supplies the vocabulary for this chapter's new fifth map layer. Goffman's focused/unfocused interaction (SC-037), already introduced in Chapter 11, is referenced here to note that a network map built from observed communication patterns risks counting unfocused copresence (e.g., shared channel membership) as though it were a meaningful tie, when it may not represent any focused interaction at all.

**Updated in v0.3, following full-text verification (TASK_CLAUDE_0015; see `SC_039_FULL_TEXT_VERIFICATION_v0.1.md`):** Fine & van den Scott's "wispy communities" (SC-039) is a sociological typology of transient leisure and fan-culture gatherings (club form vs. occasion form; occasion split into event and gathering; gatherings further split into macro "nations" such as Woodstock and Burning Man and micro "fests" such as Lebowski Fest and chess tournaments) - not a study of organizations. This chapter uses that typology as the basis for a **Palma-original caution**, extended by this book to organizational mapping: a map can reify a transient gathering as a stable structure simply because it was visible enough to be drawn. The article itself makes no claim about organizations, mapping, or workplaces; this extension is this chapter's own, and is now stated as such directly in the Draft Chapter text below.

## 6. Proposed Architecture

This chapter proposes five layers of mapping, explicitly not sequential stages but complementary, independently useful views of the same underlying relationship system, and explicitly not a single comprehensive "master map":

1. **Stakeholder map** - who has a stake in a given decision or domain, and how much their interests should weigh.
2. **Relationship map** - which of those stakeholders the organization has an identifiable relationship with at all, and at what depth.
3. **Network map** - how those relationships connect to each other, independent of the organization: clusters, bridges, structural holes, apparent hubs, isolates, bottlenecks.
4. **Flow map** - what actually moves through the identified structure: information, trust, access, and support, mapped as distinct flows.
5. **Microgroup and boundary map** (new in v0.2) - where recurrently-interacting microgroups exist, whether they are visible to the organization at all, and where symbolic and social boundaries fall around them, mapped as two distinct lines rather than one.

No single map integrates all five layers into one comprehensive picture. Each layer answers a different question; using one layer to answer a question another layer was built to answer is a specific, avoidable error this chapter names directly.

## 13. Draft Chapter

Ask most organizations to "map their stakeholders" and they will produce a grid: names down one axis, importance and influence scored along the other, colored by quadrant. This is a legitimate and useful exercise, and it answers a real question - whose interests should weigh most heavily in a given decision. It does not answer a different question this chapter is specifically about: where does this person or group actually sit in the structure of relationships the organization depends on. Importance and position are not the same property, and treating a stakeholder grid as though it were a network map is one of the most common and consequential confusions this chapter exists to correct.

A stakeholder can be important and structurally peripheral. A stakeholder can be structurally central and comparatively unimportant to any single decision. David Krackhardt and Jeffrey Hanson's practitioner research on informal networks supplies the sharpest version of this problem inside an organization's own walls: the formal chart shows reporting lines, not function. The advice network, the trust network, and the communication network are three separate, separately mappable structures that do not overlap perfectly.

This has a direct, practical implication for the "map vs. diagnosis" distinction this chapter treats as essential. A network map shows a structure. It does not, by itself, say what that structure means or what should change about it. An isolate might represent a genuine gap the organization should close, or might represent someone whose role legitimately does not require dense relational integration - the map cannot tell the difference; interpretation has to supply it.

No case has been inserted here, consistent with the instruction not to invent or infer details standing in for a real example.]*

Building a network map at all requires a shared vocabulary for the shapes it reveals: a **cluster**, a **bridge**, a **structural hole**, a **hub**, an **isolate**, a **bottleneck**. None of these terms describes a fixed, permanent property; all of them describe a position within a specific mapped structure, at a specific point in time, built from a specific and necessarily incomplete data-collection process.

A fifth layer belongs alongside the four this chapter's earlier draft already established, and TASK_CLAUDE_0012 specifically asked for it: a map of microgroups and the boundaries around them. This is a different exercise from mapping stakeholders, relationships, network structure, or flow, and it requires a distinction the other four layers do not: the difference between a symbolic boundary and a social boundary. Michèle Lamont and Virág Molnár's research on boundaries in the social sciences supplies the vocabulary. A symbolic boundary is a perceived distinction - a group of people who think of themselves, or are thought of, as a distinct "us," whether or not that perception changes anyone's actual access to anything. A social boundary is an objectified pattern of unequal access to resources or opportunity. The two are related but not identical: a symbolic boundary can exist with no corresponding social boundary at all - a group with a strong shared identity and no actual advantage or disadvantage attached to it - and a social boundary can exist with no symbolic boundary anyone has named - an access disparity that persists precisely because no one has identified or discussed it as a group-level pattern. Mapping only one of these and assuming the other follows is a specific, avoidable error. An organization that maps a symbolic boundary (people describe themselves as a distinct group) and treats that as evidence of exclusion may be solving a problem that does not structurally exist. An organization that ignores a real, mapped social boundary because no group has named itself around it is missing something a symbolic-boundary-only map would never surface.

This fifth layer carries a specific caution the other four do not carry with equal force, and this chapter states it directly rather than leaving it for a general privacy discussion to cover implicitly: mapping a microgroup's boundary risks converting something informal - a boundary the group itself holds, uses, and can change - into something formal and organization-imposed, simply by the act of drawing it on an official map and circulating that map. A boundary that existed as a felt, negotiable, member-held distinction can become a fixed, organizationally-recognized category the moment it appears on a map that management reviews - and that formalization can happen even when no one intended it, simply because official documents carry a kind of permanence and authority that informal group self-understanding does not. This chapter's position is not that microgroup and boundary mapping should never be done. It is that this specific layer should be treated with more restricted circulation, more explicit purpose limitation, and more direct involvement of the people being mapped than the other four layers - a higher bar than this chapter's general privacy discussion sets for stakeholder, relationship, network, or flow maps.

**(v0.3 correction)** A related caution applies to any map that captures a group at a single point in time: some of what a map captures may not be a stable structure at all. Sociologists Gary Alan Fine and Lisa-Jo van den Scott study what they call "wispy communities" - a typology of transient gatherings, built and illustrated entirely through leisure and fan-culture examples: an ongoing "club form" (a weekly poker game, a chess club, a mushroom-collecting society) contrasted with a rarer "occasion form," which they split into brief, performance-focused "events" and more liminal, identity-generating "gatherings" - which they further divide into large-scale "nations" (such as Woodstock or Burning Man) and small-scale "fests" (such as Lebowski Fest or chess tournaments). Their central finding is that these transient forms are genuinely meaningful to participants while active, and then become latent, without this being a failure or an immature stage of something more durable. Fine and van den Scott do not study organizations, workplaces, or network mapping at all - their examples are exclusively leisure and fan-culture gatherings. Applying their typology to organizational mapping, as this chapter does, is a **Palma extension**, not a conclusion the authors draw: this chapter's caution is that an organization's map can reify a group that happened to be visible and mappable at the moment of mapping as though its visibility proved its durability, when in fact some organizational gatherings may be legitimately transient in exactly the sense Fine and van den Scott describe for leisure gatherings. A map is a snapshot. Some of what it captures will still be there next quarter. Some of it was never going to be, and mapping it does not change that - it only risks making an organization believe otherwise.

Mapping what flows through a structure is a further, separate step from mapping the structure itself. Information, trust, access, and support do not travel identically through the same channels.

*(The chapter's existing discussion of flow-mapping, incomplete-data caution, privacy obligations, and qualitative/quantitative method-mixing from v0.1 is preserved unchanged here; see `CHAPTER_12_v0.1.md` for the full unmodified text of that material, retained because TASK_CLAUDE_0012 did not identify a problem requiring its revision.)*

Finally, this chapter carries forward, deliberately and without softening, a caution already established elsewhere in this book: much of the most quantitatively rigorous network science was developed for very large, open-growth networks, not the small, bounded stakeholder networks most organizations actually have. Using these findings as an illustrative lens is legitimate. Asserting that a specific, small, measured organizational network is "scale-free" is not.
