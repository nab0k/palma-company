# Chapter 21 — Handover (packet)

**Maps to:** the ownership/capability principle running across Enable and Evaluate. Explicitly not a seventh phase (Palma Method Canon v1.0 §3, §6).

## Purpose

Name, as its own subject rather than a byproduct of Enable and Evaluate, the single question that determines whether an engagement actually succeeded on Palma's own terms: who is able to run this work when Palma is no longer in the room. This chapter exists to stop Handover from being read as an event (a meeting, a document, a formal sign-off) and establish it instead as a standard that Enable and Evaluate are already being held to throughout.

## Central argument

Handover is not a phase because it does not have its own inputs, activities, and exit criteria the way Orient through Evaluate do — it is a lens applied to two of them. Enable is where capability transfer is attempted; Evaluate is where whether it actually took is checked, alongside everything else Evaluate assesses. Treating Handover as a seventh, separate step invites exactly the failure this chapter has to warn against: a closing ceremony that feels like ownership has transferred, decoupled from any real test of whether it has. The chapter's job is to fix the reader's attention on capability and ownership as a running question asked at every point after Design, not a box checked once near the end.

## Phase mapping

Not a phase in DECISION_0005's architecture. Cross-references: Enable's commitment/efficacy test (Chapter 19) is the first place this principle is operationalized; Evaluate's adaptive-relational-capability layer (Chapter 20, Core §10 Layer 5) is the second. Handover, as a chapter, draws the line between them explicit rather than leaving readers to infer it.

## Claims / evidence table

| Claim | Evidentiary basis | Status |
|---|---|---|
| A consultant relationship can produce dependence rather than capability if the consultant does not actively design against it | Schein (process consultation — the consultant must not substitute for the client's own diagnosis); Argyris & Schön (single-loop learning can look like success while leaving the client unable to question the norms that produced the original problem) | Well-supported, foundational consulting literature |
| Ownership transfer is better verified through demonstrated explanation of mechanism than through document handoff or verbal sign-off | Synthesis from Weiner's readiness-for-change framework (Chapter 19) applied specifically to the ownership question | Palma's own application of established theory; not independently tested as a transfer-verification method |
| Platform- or founder-dependent structures with no succession path are a recognized organizational risk, not a hypothetical one | Schneider, *Governable Spaces* (2024); creator-economy and brand-community research on platform-dependent versus community-owned models (2024–2025) | Well-supported as a documented, currently-recognized pattern |

## Practical tools

The commitment/efficacy split (Chapter 19) read specifically for what it says about ownership, not just about pilot readiness; the Adaptive Relational Capability layer of the outcome model (Chapter 20) read as the long-horizon version of the same question; the Stop Rule concerning founder, manager, or platform dependence with no addressed succession path, applied at any point, not only at formal handover.

## Counterarguments

A reader could reasonably ask why this material needs its own chapter if it is, by this chapter's own argument, not a separate phase — the risk of the chapter dissolving into a restatement of Chapters 19 and 20 is real and should be named. The answer the chapter has to earn, not assert, is that cross-cutting principles get lost precisely because they are not tied to a single phase's checklist, and a book organized phase-by-phase needs at least one place that makes the connection explicit. A second, sharper objection: an organization can look capable at the moment of formal handover and lose that capability later through turnover or changed priorities — no handover practice, however well-designed, guarantees durability past that point.

## Risks of overclaiming

This chapter must not suggest that passing Enable's capability test and Evaluate's adaptive-relational-capability layer together constitute proof that dependence has been avoided — both are point-in-time checks, and organizational capability can erode afterward for reasons outside Palma's visibility or control. It must not present "handover" as a deliverable Palma can certify; it can only describe the tests applied and their result at the time they were applied.

## Connections

Backward to Chapters 19 and 20, whose material this chapter re-reads through a single lens rather than restates. Forward to the Conclusion, which needs this chapter's honesty about the limits of any handover practice to avoid overclaiming what the method as a whole can guarantee.

## Draft structure (for the manuscript prose)

1. Open by naming the temptation Handover creates: an event that feels conclusive.
2. Argue for treating it as a standard applied twice (Enable, Evaluate) rather than a step in its own right.
3. Ground the standard in the consulting literature's own long-standing worry about manufactured dependence.
4. Name the succession/platform-dependence risk as the sharpest concrete version of the same problem.
5. Close on the limits: no handover practice guarantees durability past the point it was checked.

## Sources

Schein (1999, 2013); Argyris & Schön (1974/1996); Weiner (readiness for change); Schneider (2024) — full citations as verified in Palma Canon v0.1 and the Management & Facilitation Practice Layer v0.1.

## Verification gaps

No longitudinal evidence exists, in material available to this task, on whether Palma-verified capability transfer actually holds over time once an engagement ends. This is an honest limit of the method as documented, not a gap this chapter can close.

## Editorial decisions

Written as a genuinely cross-cutting chapter rather than a compressed repeat of Chapters 19–20; where the underlying working-folder material grounded this principle in a specific handover plan from the restricted case file, that grounding is replaced here with the general pattern it illustrated (a plan can show commitment without having been tested for efficacy), stated without attribution to any case.
