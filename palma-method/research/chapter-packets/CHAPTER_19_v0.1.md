# Chapter 19 — Launch (packet)

**Maps to core phase:** Enable (Palma Method Canon v1.0 §3, §6).

## Purpose

Establish that a design becoming real is not the same event as a client becoming capable of running it. This chapter has to correct the most tempting shortcut in the whole method: treating a live pilot as proof that the work is done, when a pilot only tests whether the conditions can exist, not whether the organization can sustain them without Palma in the room.

## Central argument

Enable is where Adaptive Relational Capability is either built or quietly skipped. A handover document, however well-built, transfers instructions; it does not by itself transfer understanding of why an instruction works. The chapter's argument is that capability has two independent components — commitment (does the client want to sustain this, shown through action) and efficacy (can the client explain, unprompted, why a given action is supposed to work) — and that a plan can pass one while failing the other. Treating "the client agreed and seems enthusiastic" as sufficient evidence of Enable being complete is the specific failure this chapter has to rule out.

## Phase mapping

**Enable:** bounded interventions, participation, distributed capability, adaptation in use (DECISION_0005). Operationally: hand over the tools used to build the diagnosis and design, not just their conclusions; launch designed conditions as pilots rather than full rollouts where feasible; build a learning and adjustment routine with a named reviewer and schedule; verify commitment and efficacy separately before treating the phase as closed.

## Claims / evidence table

| Claim | Evidentiary basis | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Organizational readiness for change has two independent components (commitment and efficacy) | Weiner's theory of organizational readiness for change, actively extended in 2023–2024 research | Well-supported, active research line |
| Short-term wins only sustain change if visible and correctly attributed, and momentum is the step most commonly abandoned after a first win | Kotter's eight-step change model, particularly the later steps | Well-established in change-management literature; the "most commonly abandoned" framing is a practitioner observation repeated across the literature, not a single measured statistic |
| Groups pass through a genuinely uncomfortable middle phase before real convergence, and rushing past it produces brittle agreement | Kaner's Diamond of Participation and the "Groan Zone" | Well-established in facilitation practice |
| How a group is configured (who speaks, in what structure) shapes outcome quality more than the content of the agenda | Lipmanowicz & McCandless, Liberating Structures | Practitioner-developed framework with wide adoption; not a controlled-trial evidence base |
| A fluent recitation of a plan is not evidence that the person reciting it understands the mechanism | General finding from the change-readiness and facilitation literature above, applied as a synthesis, not itself independently tested | Reasonable inference from established sources; the specific test (ask the client to explain why one action works) is Palma's own operationalization |

## Practical tools

The two-part capability test replacing a single pass/fail check (Management & Facilitation Practice Layer, patch to Protocol Phase 5); Kotter's later steps reframed as the mechanism for a learning routine, not an add-on; Kaner's Groan Zone as a reason to treat discomfort in an internal transfer session as a sign of real integration, not a sign the session is failing; Liberating Structures as a default alternative to a status-report-style handover meeting, especially when more than one person needs the capability transferred or efficacy is in doubt.

## Counterarguments

A reasonable objection: insisting on a demonstrated explanation of mechanism, not just a completed checklist, asks more of a client relationship than most commercial engagements budget time for, and risks reading as the consultant testing the client rather than serving them. The chapter should take this seriously rather than dismiss it — the answer is that the alternative (leaving efficacy unverified) produces engagements that look successful and regress once attention moves elsewhere, which is a worse outcome for the client, not a more efficient one. A second objection: Kotter's model was built for large-scale organizational change, and applying its later steps to a bounded Palma pilot may overstate what a small intervention needs.

## Risks of overclaiming

The chapter must not suggest that passing the two-part capability test guarantees a design will be sustained — Enable can be run correctly and the organization can still let the practice lapse for reasons outside the design's control (turnover, competing priorities, a change in leadership). It must not claim Kotter's or Kaner's frameworks were built for, or validated on, engagements of Palma's typical scale; they are imported general theory, applied by analogy. No specific pilot outcome should be described, since none can be cited without the restricted case material.

## Draft structure (for the manuscript prose)

1. Open on the seduction of a live pilot as proof of success.
2. Why a handover document transfers instructions, not understanding.
3. Splitting the capability test: commitment and efficacy as separable, separately checkable things.
4. The craft of the transfer session itself — discomfort as a sign of integration, not failure.
5. What "Enable is not yet complete" looks like in practice, and why that is a normal, not exceptional, finding.

## Sources

Weiner (organizational readiness for change, 2023–2024 extensions); Kotter, *Leading Change*; Kaner et al., Diamond of Participation; Lipmanowicz & McCandless, *Liberating Structures* — full citations as verified in the Management & Facilitation Practice Layer v0.1.

## Verification gaps

No independent measurement instrument exists for Palma's specific commitment/efficacy test; it is a direct application of Weiner's framework, not a validated tool in its own right. The claim that most engagements fail at the "sustain momentum" step rather than elsewhere in the sequence has not been quantified for Palma's own engagement base.

## Editorial decisions

Written without the handover-plan example that originally grounded this material in the restricted case file (a day-by-day, time-boxed plan matched to a client's available hours). The general shape of that finding — a concrete plan can show commitment without having tested efficacy — is preserved as a stated pattern, not as a citation to any specific engagement.
