core_claim: >
  Small, recurrently-interacting groups generate their own **idioculture**: a system of shared
  knowledge, beliefs, behaviors, and customs unique to that group, built from a common fund of
  experience and referenced by members to construct group identity. Idioculture is a real,
  observable output of repeated interaction, not just a byproduct - it becomes a resource members
  use to coordinate, include, exclude, and signal belonging.
summary: >
  Based on ethnographic fieldwork with Little League baseball teams, Fine shows that even short-
  lived, non-elective small groups (children assigned to a team for one season) rapidly develop an
  idioculture - in-jokes, nicknames, shared narratives, behavioral norms - that outsiders (including
  parents and coaches on the periphery) cannot access without participating in the group's history.
  This is a foundational empirical demonstration that "culture" is not only a property of large,
  stable institutions; it is generated locally, quickly, and durably by small groups through
  recurring interaction, and it functions as a boundary-marking and coordination resource.
key_evidence: >

  and tracing their diffusion and function within each team.
