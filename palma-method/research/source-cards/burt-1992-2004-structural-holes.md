core_claim: >
  Social capital is a function of brokerage across "structural holes" - gaps between clusters of
  people who are not otherwise connected. People (or organizations) whose networks span structural
  holes get earlier access to more diverse, non-redundant information, control over information
  flow between groups, and - per the 2004 study - measurably better ideas and faster promotion.
summary: >
  Burt reframes Granovetter's tie-strength argument in structural terms: what matters is not
  whether a tie is "weak" but whether it bridges an otherwise-unconnected gap (a structural hole).
  Ties to multiple people within the same tightly-knit cluster are redundant - the marginal value
  of the second, third, and fourth tie into the same cluster drops sharply. Burt's later work
  (2000, 2005) contrasts brokerage (Burt's structural-holes logic) with closure (Coleman's dense-
  network logic): closure builds trust and enforces norms; brokerage generates novel information
  and creative recombination. Organizations and individuals need both, applied deliberately, not
  reflexively maximized.
key_evidence: >
  2004 study of ~673 managers in a large U.S. electronics company: managers whose networks spanned
  structural holes were more likely to have their ideas rated as valuable by peers, received higher
  compensation, and were more likely to be promoted, controlling for other factors.
important_quote: >
  "It is not the intelligence of individual managers that gives them a competitive edge, but the
  structure of their surrounding social networks - specifically, whether their networks span
  structural holes that give them access to diverse information and control over its flow."

limitations: >
  Findings are strongest in settings where information advantage translates directly into
  organizational reward (large hierarchical firms); applicability to smaller organizations,
  professional-services or founder-led contexts is less directly tested. Brokerage advantage can
  also produce "broker exploitation" dynamics - see anti-Palma track.
counterarguments: >
  Coleman-style closure theorists argue brokerage-heavy positions, if not paired with some closure,
  produce fragile trust and can be perceived as opportunistic ("playing both sides"). Uzzi (1997)
  shows embedded, closure-style ties can outperform pure brokerage in contexts requiring fine-
  grained trust and information transfer (e.g. apparel-industry supply chains).
