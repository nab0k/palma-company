core_claim: >
  Harley-Davidson's Harley Owners Group (HOG), launched 1983, is a widely cited business-press
  example of a company successfully converting a passive customer base into a genuine,
  member-driven community rather than a broadcast audience - directly illustrative of the book's
  Chapter 1-2 argument that an audience (people who receive messages) is structurally different
  from a community (people who have relationships with each other, not just with the brand).
  Harley-Davidson was near bankruptcy in the 1980s when it began deliberately investing in
  community infrastructure (chapters, rallies, member-organized rides) rather than only
  advertising, and HOG became one of the most-cited "brand community done right" cases in
  management literature since.
summary: >
  This HBR article (part of a broader literature on brand communities, following Muniz and
  O'Guinn's foundational 2001 "Brand Community" academic construct) discusses principles for
  building brand communities that create genuine value rather than staged marketing activity. The
  Harley-Davidson case is one of the most frequently cited applied examples across marketing and
  management literature: HOG chapters are largely member-organized and member-led, with the
  company providing infrastructure and legitimacy rather than being the primary generator of
  member-to-member activity - the inverse of the failure pattern FeverBee's Richard Millington
  describes (cited in the anti-Palma argument map) where brand communities die once the company's
  own staff-generated activity is the majority of visible engagement.
