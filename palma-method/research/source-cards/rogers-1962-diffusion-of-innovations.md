core_claim: >
  New ideas, practices, or behaviors spread through a social system in a predictable pattern shaped
  by adopter characteristics, not primarily by the intrinsic quality of the idea alone. Rogers
  identifies five adopter categories along an adoption curve - innovators (~2.5%), early adopters
  (~13.5%), early majority (~34%), late majority (~34%), and laggards (~16%) - and argues successful
  diffusion depends heavily on early adopters specifically, who are more socially integrated and
  more trusted by the mainstream than innovators are, and whose visible adoption confers legitimacy
  that pulls the early and late majority in.
summary: >
  Rogers synthesized decades of diffusion research (originally agricultural-extension studies, later
  extended across health, technology, and organizational contexts) into a general theory: adoption
  of anything new follows an S-shaped curve over time, and the rate and eventual reach of adoption
  depend on five characteristics of the innovation itself (relative advantage, compatibility,
  complexity, trialability, observability) and on the social structure of the adopting system,
  particularly the role of opinion leaders. Early adopters are structurally distinct from innovators:
  innovators are often peripheral, risk-tolerant, and less embedded in the mainstream social system,
  while early adopters are well-integrated, respected members of their community whose choices are
  actively watched and imitated by the larger, more risk-averse early and late majority. This
  structural distinction - not simply "who tries things first" - is the mechanism Rogers identifies
  for why targeting early adopters (rather than innovators) is usually the more effective diffusion
  strategy.

limitations: >
  Originally developed largely from agricultural-extension and public-health contexts (e.g., adoption
  of new farming techniques, family-planning practices); applying the adopter-category percentages
  and curve shape to organizational relationship-building or internal culture-change contexts is a
  well-established but still adapted extension, not something Rogers tested directly in that setting.
  The specific percentage breakdowns (2.5% / 13.5% / 34% / 34% / 16%) are a normalized statistical
  idealization (based on a normal distribution assumption) rather than an exact prediction for any
  specific real population, and should be presented as an illustrative pattern, not a precise
  forecast for any given organization.
counterarguments: >
  Some diffusion researchers argue the theory understates the role of active promotion,
  organizational power, and resource asymmetry in adoption (i.e., diffusion is not always organic -
  it can be manufactured by whoever controls distribution or incentives), and that treating adoption
  as primarily an individual psychological/social process can obscure structural barriers (cost,
  access, policy) that have little to do with opinion leadership.
