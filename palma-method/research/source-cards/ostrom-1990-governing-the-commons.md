core_claim: >
  Groups can, and routinely do, self-organize to manage shared resources sustainably without
  either top-down state control or full privatization - contrary to the "tragedy of the commons"
  prediction. Ostrom identifies eight recurring design principles present in long-enduring
  self-governing institutions.
summary: >
  Direct empirical rebuttal to Garrett Hardin's "tragedy of the commons" thesis and to standard
  rational-choice predictions that shared resources are always over-exploited absent external
  enforcement. Based on comparative case studies of durable commons institutions (Swiss alpine
  grazing, Japanese village forests, Spanish and Filipino irrigation systems), Ostrom derives eight
  design principles: (1) clearly defined boundaries; (2) rules matched to local conditions; (3)
  collective-choice arrangements (those affected can participate in rule-making); (4) monitoring
  by or accountable to appropriators; (5) graduated sanctions; (6) fast, low-cost conflict
  resolution; (7) recognized right to self-organize; (8) nested enterprises for larger systems.
  Ostrom won the 2009 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for this work.
key_evidence: >
  Comparative institutional case analysis across multiple long-enduring (centuries-old, in some
  cases) common-pool-resource institutions, contrasted with documented failure cases lacking these
  design features.
