core_claim: >
  Despite decades of public relations and communication theory framing the field as "two-way" and
  "dialogic," organizations overwhelmingly practice one-way speaking rather than listening.
  Macnamara's multi-year Organizational Listening Project found that organizations devote a
  disproportionate share of their communication resources and infrastructure to disseminating
  messages, with comparatively little formal capability, budget, or accountability built for
  listening - meaning "listening" is often rhetorical rather than structurally real.
summary: >
  Based on a study of 36 corporate, government, and NGO organizations across Australia, the UK, and
  the US (104 interviews plus content analysis of 400+ documents), Macnamara found organizations
  reported spending on average around 80% (in some cases up to 95%) of communication-related
  resources on speaking/disseminating rather than listening. He argues this imbalance is structural.
  Most organizations lack a dedicated listening function, budget line, or role with the same status
  as messaging/content functions; "listening" activities that do exist (social monitoring, surveys,
  comment forms) are frequently used to inform outbound messaging rather than to genuinely change
  organizational decisions. Macnamara distinguishes listening from merely collecting data about
  stakeholders: listening implies the organization is structurally capable of being changed by what
  it hears, not simply capable of recording it.
key_evidence: >
  Qualitative, interview- and document-based organizational study (not an experimental or
  statistical design); consistent, convergent finding across a geographically and sectorally
  diverse sample of 36 organizations, triangulated with document analysis rather than resting on
  self-report alone.
