core_claim: >
  Employee voice (voluntarily communicating suggestions, concerns, or opinions upward) and silence
  (withholding that same input) are distinct, not simply opposite ends of one behavior, and both are
  shaped less by whether employees have something to say than by whether they judge it safe and
  worthwhile to say it. Silence is not evidence of absence of problems or ideas - it is frequently
  evidence of a rational risk calculation employees make about speaking up.
summary: >
  Morrison's review synthesizes the organizational-behavior literature on why employees do or do not
  speak up to those in higher positions. Voice is shaped by perceived risk (fear of being seen as
  negative, incompetent, or disloyal), perceived efficacy (will speaking up actually change
  anything), and organizational/supervisory climate (psychological safety, leader openness, power
  distance). Silence is not simply low voice - it is often an active, effortful withholding driven
  by the same risk calculus, and it clusters systematically: employees lower in organizational
  power, newer employees, and members of underrepresented groups are disproportionately more likely
  to stay silent even when they hold relevant information, meaning silence is not randomly
  distributed and cannot be treated as a proxy for "nothing important to report."
