core_claim: >
  Learning is fundamentally social, not individual - it happens through participation in a
  "community of practice": a group that shares a domain of interest, interacts regularly
  (community), and develops a shared repertoire of practice over time. Identity and competence are
  built through this participation, moving from peripheral to fuller engagement.
summary: >
  A community of practice requires three structural elements: (1) domain - a shared area of
  interest that gives the group identity and purpose; (2) community - relationships built through
  joint activities, discussion, and mutual engagement, not just shared interest; (3) practice - a
  shared repertoire of resources (experiences, stories, tools, ways of addressing problems)
  developed over time. Wenger's four dimensions of learning as social participation: learning as
  doing (practice), learning as experience (meaning), learning as belonging (community), and
  learning as becoming (identity). Newcomers begin at "legitimate peripheral participation" and
  move toward fuller membership as they contribute.
key_evidence: >

  apprenticeship contexts such as Yucatec midwives, tailors, and naval quartermasters in the 1991
  book; later extended to organizational and corporate knowledge-sharing contexts in the 1998 book
  and subsequent consulting practice).
important_quote: >
