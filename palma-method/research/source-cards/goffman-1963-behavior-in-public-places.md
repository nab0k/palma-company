core_claim: >
  Face-to-face social life is organized into distinguishable, analyzable units smaller than "group"
  or "community." An **encounter** (or focused interaction) is a bounded episode in which two or
  more people sustain a single focus of cognitive and visual attention, governed by tacit rules of
  entry, exit, and relevance - a smaller, more fundamental unit than a microgroup or organization.
  **Unfocused interaction** - the mutual monitoring and tacit regulation (e.g., civil inattention)
  that occurs between copresent people who are not jointly focused on anything - is a distinct,
  necessary backdrop that focused encounters emerge from and return to.
summary: >
  Goffman's studies of public order argue that social organization exists at a level beneath formal
  groups and institutions: the moment-to-moment management of copresence itself. A focused
  interaction (encounter) has its own internal rules - who may enter, how attention is sustained,
  how the encounter is closed - independent of the participants' formal relationship or
  organizational roles. This matters directly for any book chapter concerned with "listening,"
  "mapping," or "interaction," because it supplies the smallest well-theorized unit of analysis
  below the level of a relationship or a microgroup: not every interaction is a relationship, and
  not every gathering is a group, but the interaction order still has observable structure that
  affects what information moves, who is included, and who is systematically bypassed.
