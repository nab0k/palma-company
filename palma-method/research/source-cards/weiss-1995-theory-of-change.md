core_claim: >
  Complex social and organizational initiatives are difficult to evaluate not primarily because
  evaluation methods are weak, but because the initiatives themselves rarely make their own
  underlying assumptions explicit - what has to happen first, second, and third for the intended
  long-term result to occur, and why. Without surfacing this implicit "theory of change," evaluation
  can only measure whether an ultimate goal was reached, not why it was or was not, which prevents
  organizations from learning and adapting mid-course.
summary: >
  Weiss argued that most complex community and organizational initiatives are built on implicit
  theories - assumptions about how and why a given set of activities will lead to a given long-term
  outcome - that are rarely written down or examined. She proposed that evaluators work with program
  designers to make these assumptions explicit as a chain of if-then propositions (a "theory of
  change"), specifying intermediate, observable markers of progress along the way, not just the
  final outcome. This allows evaluation to test the theory at each link in the chain, rather than
  only asking "did the final goal happen" - which, for long-horizon, multi-causal initiatives, is
  frequently unable to distinguish a good theory poorly implemented from a bad theory well
  implemented. This distinction is the conceptual root of the field's later attribution-vs-contribution
  debate: for genuinely multi-causal outcomes, evaluation should assess whether an initiative
  plausibly contributed to a result via its stated theory of change, rather than claiming to have
  proven it was the sole cause (attribution), which is rarely achievable outside controlled
  experimental conditions.
