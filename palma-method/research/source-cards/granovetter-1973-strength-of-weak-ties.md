core_claim: >
  Weak ties (acquaintances) are, paradoxically, often more valuable than strong ties (close
  friends/family) for spreading novel information and opportunity, because weak ties are more
  likely to bridge otherwise disconnected social clusters. "No strong tie is a bridge" - dense
  clusters of strong ties tend to circulate redundant information.
summary: >
  Granovetter's central mechanism: the strength of a tie correlates with the overlap of the two
  people's networks (strong ties cluster into dense, mutually-connected groups; weak ties are
  more likely to reach outside that cluster). Because information circulating within a dense
  cluster of strong ties becomes redundant quickly, genuinely new information - about jobs,
  opportunities, ideas - tends to arrive via weak ties that connect separate clusters. His
  empirical study of professional/managerial job changers in a Boston suburb found the majority
  got their job through a contact seen "occasionally" or "rarely," not through close contacts.
key_evidence: >
  Survey of ~282 professional, technical, and managerial job changers in Newton, Massachusetts:
  of those who found jobs through personal contacts, a majority reported the contact was someone
  they saw only occasionally or rarely, not a close friend.
important_quote: >
  "Weak ties are more likely to link members of different small groups than are strong ties, which
  tend to be concentrated within particular groups."
quote_page_or_location: "p. 1376"
methodology: "network-structural theory paper with an original empirical labor-market survey"
sample: "~282 professional/managerial job changers, Newton, MA, USA (early 1970s)"
limitations: >
  Single-city, single-occupational-stratum sample from the early 1970s U.S. labor market; findings
  on job search have been replicated broadly but the magnitude and mechanism vary by labor market

  platform-mediated hiring.
counterarguments: >
  Burt's structural-holes work reframes the mechanism (it's the absence of connection between
  contacts - the "hole" - that matters, not tie strength per se; some strong ties can also bridge
  holes). Krackhardt (1992) argues "strength of strong ties" matters for trust and cooperation
  under uncertainty, complicating a simple "weak ties are better" reading.
