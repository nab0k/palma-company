core_claim: >
  A **community of fate** is a social bond formed not by choice, shared values, or cultivated
  relationship, but by an externally imposed shared circumstance - a common exposure to risk, crisis,
  or consequence (the classical German term is Schicksalsgemeinschaft, "community of fate/destiny").
  This is a categorically different mechanism of group formation than the recurring-interaction and
  designed-relationship mechanisms addressed elsewhere in this brief: people can become bonded, feel
  solidarity, and act collectively because they share exposure to the same threat or outcome, without
  ever having chosen the relationship or built it through repeated positive interaction.
summary: >
  The concept traces to early 20th-century German sociology (Weber and Simmel both use versions of
  "community of fate" language in discussing how shared, often involuntary, circumstances - war,
  economic crisis, catastrophe - can generate group solidarity independent of prior relationship or
  shared values). Modern treatments, including work by Peter Baehr discussing collective responses to
  crises such as SARS, extend this to show how organizations, neighborhoods, or populations can be
  bonded together suddenly by shared exposure to a threat, producing rapid, sometimes intense,
  often short-lived solidarity that does not follow the slow accretion patterns described elsewhere
  in this literature (e.g., Fine's idioculture, SC-038). This matters for the book because it
  identifies a bonding mechanism (shared external circumstance) that is neither designed by an
  organization nor built through voluntary recurring interaction - it is imposed by events.
