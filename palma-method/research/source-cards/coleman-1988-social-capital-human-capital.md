core_claim: >
  Social capital is a resource that inheres not in individuals or in physical/financial capital,
  but in the structure of relations between and among actors. It is productive, making possible
  the achievement of ends that would otherwise be unattainable, and it functions like a public
  good - benefiting everyone embedded in the structure, not just the person who "invested" in it.
summary: >
  Coleman identifies three forms of social capital: (1) obligations, expectations, and
  trustworthiness of structures (a "credit slip" system where favors are owed and repaid); (2)
  information channels embedded in relationships; and (3) norms and effective sanctions that
  constrain or encourage particular kinds of behavior (e.g., norms favoring collective welfare
  over self-interest). Unlike Bourdieu, Coleman frames social capital in rational-choice terms -
  actors invest in relationships because doing so serves their interests - but the resulting
  capital is jointly held and non-excludable within the group, closer to a public good. Empirical
  illustration: closure in parent networks (all parents knowing each other) reduces high-school
  dropout rates net of family background, because it enables norm enforcement and information
  sharing that no single parent could achieve alone.
key_evidence: >
  Uses High School and Beyond longitudinal data; Catholic-school students had substantially lower
  dropout rates than public-school students with similar backgrounds, attributed partly to denser
  "intergenerational closure" among the adult community around the school.
important_quote: >
  "Social capital is defined by its function. It is not a single entity but a variety of different
  entities having two characteristics in common: they all consist of some aspect of a social
  structure, and they facilitate certain actions of individuals who are within the structure."

limitations: >
  Public-good framing can obscure unequal access within a structure (a criticism raised by later
  scholars, e.g. Portes 1998). Closure-based social capital (Coleman) can also produce
  conformity pressure and reduced innovation - a tension with Burt's structural-holes argument
  that closure and brokerage are opposing logics.
counterarguments: >
  Burt (2000, 2004) argues that closure and brokerage are two different, sometimes competing,
  mechanisms of social capital - closure controls behavior and enforces trust, brokerage
  generates novel information and opportunity. A relationship system optimized purely for closure
  (Coleman) may reduce the innovation and access benefits described by Burt and Granovetter.
business_implication: >
  Gives Palma a rigorous basis for the claim that dense, closed relationship structures (e.g. tight
  internal teams, long-standing client circles) produce real value through trust and norm
  enforcement, but that this value is different from - and can trade off against - the
  information/opportunity value that comes from bridging outward (see Granovetter, Burt).
palma_implication: >
  Directly supports the "Design" stage of the Five-Finger Model: designers of relationship systems
  must decide whether they are building for closure (trust, reliability, mutual monitoring) or for
  bridging (novel information, access, opportunity) - most organizations default to neither and get
  the benefits of nothing.
