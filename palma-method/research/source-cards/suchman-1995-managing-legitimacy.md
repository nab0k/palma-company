core_claim: >
  Legitimacy is a distinct construct from reputation, trust, or awareness: "a generalized
  perception or assumption that the actions of an entity are desirable, proper, or appropriate
  within some socially constructed system of norms, values, beliefs, and definitions." Suchman
  identifies three forms: pragmatic legitimacy (based on the audience's self-interested
  calculation of what the entity does for them), moral legitimacy (based on normative approval -
  is this the right thing to do), and cognitive legitimacy (based on comprehensibility and
  taken-for-grantedness - does this entity's existence and behavior even register as sensible
  and expected, prior to any evaluation of it).
summary: >
  This is a foundational, highly cited (3,200+ citations) synthesis of the organizational
  legitimacy literature, reconciling strategic (management-choice-driven) and institutional
  (environment-driven) approaches to how organizations gain, maintain, and repair legitimacy.
  Suchman's core contribution for this book's purposes is the definitional distinction: legitimacy
  is about social appropriateness and fit within a normative system, not about being known
  (awareness), being liked or well-regarded (reputation), or being specifically relied upon
  (trust/access). An organization can be legitimate without being well known, and can be
  well-known without being seen as legitimate.
