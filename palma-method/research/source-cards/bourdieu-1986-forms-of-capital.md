core_claim: >
  Capital exists in three fundamental forms - economic, cultural, and social - and social capital
  is convertible into economic capital under certain conditions. Social capital is not a natural
  given; it is the product of investment strategies (individual or collective, conscious or
  unconscious) aimed at establishing or reproducing social relationships that are directly usable
  in the short or long term.
summary: >
  Bourdieu defines social capital as "the aggregate of the actual or potential resources which
  are linked to possession of a durable network of more or less institutionalised relationships
  of mutual acquaintance and recognition - or in other words, to membership in a group." Unlike
  Coleman and Putnam, Bourdieu treats social capital as a property of the individual (or family),
  not the collective, and situates it inside a broader theory of class reproduction: capital
  converts across forms (economic to social to cultural and back), and access to social capital
  tracks existing structures of power and stratification. Social capital is therefore inherently
  about advantage-seeking and inequality, not just cooperation.
key_evidence: >
  Conceptual/theoretical argument rather than empirical study. Bourdieu illustrates with examples
  of how bourgeois families convert economic capital into social connections (private schools,
  clubs, networks) which are then reconverted into economic and cultural advantage across
  generations.
important_quote: >
  "The volume of the social capital possessed by a given agent thus depends on the size of the
  network of connections he can effectively mobilize and on the volume of the capital (economic,
  cultural or symbolic) possessed in his own right by each of those to whom he is connected."
