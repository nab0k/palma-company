core_claim: >
  A firm should be understood and managed as a set of relationships with groups that can affect or
  are affected by its objectives - stakeholders - not merely as a bundle of transactions with
  shareholders. Freeman's original, still-cited definition: a stakeholder is "any group or
  individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives."
summary: >
  Freeman shifted strategic management away from a shareholder-only frame toward a relational
  frame: value creation depends on managing relationships with customers, suppliers, employees,
  communities, financiers, and other groups simultaneously, because these relationships are jointly
  interdependent rather than separable line items. Later refinements (Freeman, Harrison, Wicks,
  Parmar & de Colle, "Stakeholder Theory: The State of the Art," 2010) formalize stakeholder
  mapping as a strategic discipline: identifying stakeholders, their interests, their power/
  influence, and the nature of the firm's relationship to each. Modern stakeholder-capitalism
  discourse (World Economic Forum, McKinsey, and Business Roundtable's 2019 statement) is a direct
  descendant of this 1984 framework.
key_evidence: >
  Originally a conceptual/strategic-management framework rather than an empirical study; the
  broader stakeholder-theory research program that followed has generated substantial empirical
  literature linking stakeholder engagement quality to firm performance, though causality and
  measurement remain actively debated.
