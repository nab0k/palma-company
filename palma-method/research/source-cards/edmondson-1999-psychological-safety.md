core_claim: >
  Psychological safety - "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for
  interpersonal risk-taking" - predicts team learning behavior (asking for help, admitting error,
  seeking feedback, experimenting), which in turn predicts team performance. Counterintuitively,
  psychologically safe teams often report more errors, not fewer, because they surface problems
  rather than hide them.
summary: >
  Edmondson's field study of hospital care teams found that psychological safety varied
  significantly across teams and predicted both self-reported learning behavior and independently
  rated team performance. The mechanism: interpersonal risk (looking incompetent, ignorant,
  negative, or disruptive) inhibits the exact behaviors - speaking up, questioning, reporting
  mistakes - that organizations need for improvement. Where psychological safety is high, those
  behaviors increase and errors get caught and corrected rather than concealed; the surface-level
  metric ("more reported errors") can look worse while underlying safety and performance are
  actually better - an important measurement caution (relevant to Track F).
key_evidence: >
  Multi-team field study using survey measures of psychological safety correlated with independently
  rated team performance and observed/reported error and learning behavior; extended body of
  subsequent research (including Google's 2015-2016 "Project Aristotle" internal study) has
  replicated psychological safety as the strongest single predictor of team effectiveness among the
  factors tested.
important_quote: >

  interpersonal risk taking."
quote_page_or_location: "p. 354"
methodology: "field study with survey instruments and independently rated team performance outcomes"
sample: "hospital care teams (original 1999 study), subsequently replicated across many organizational contexts"
limitations: >
  Originally studied within intact, formal work teams inside a single organization; extending the
  construct to looser, cross-organizational stakeholder or client-community relationships (Palma's
  typical unit of design) requires adaptation, since team-level psychological safety assumes ongoing
  co-membership that a professional community may not have.
counterarguments: >
  Some critics note psychological safety can be conflated with simple niceness or conflict
  avoidance; Edmondson's later work (The Fearless Organization, 2019) explicitly distinguishes
  psychological safety from a low-standards, comfortable environment - safety plus high standards,
  not safety instead of standards, is what predicts performance.
business_implication: >
  Gives Palma a rigorous, well-validated construct for why some client stakeholder groups
  participate openly (share problems, give honest feedback, contribute ideas) while others stay
  guarded despite apparent goodwill - the missing variable is often psychological safety, not
  interest or trust in the abstract.
palma_implication: >
  Directly usable in the "Design" stage: rituals and roles that increase psychological safety
  (structured turn-taking, explicit norms against status-based punishment for speaking up,
  visible leader vulnerability) are concrete design levers for turning a passive audience into a
  contributing community.
