core_claim: >
  **Collective efficacy** - defined precisely as social cohesion among neighbors combined with their
  shared willingness to intervene on behalf of the common good - is empirically distinguishable from,
  and a stronger predictor of, lower violent crime than either simple social cohesion alone or
  aggregate demographic/structural disadvantage. Collective efficacy is a property of the
  neighborhood-level social unit, not simply the sum of individual residents' attitudes or ties.
summary: >
  Using a large multilevel survey of Chicago neighborhoods combined with independently observed and
  recorded violence data, Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls show that neighborhoods with similar levels
  of poverty and residential instability can have very different violent-crime rates depending on
  their level of collective efficacy - i.e., whether residents trust each other AND believe their
  neighbors would act (e.g., intervene if children were skipping school or a fight broke out) if
  needed. This paper is the origin of "collective efficacy" as a precise, measured sociological
  construct distinct from vaguer notions of "community spirit" or "social capital" in general.

  immigrant concentration).
