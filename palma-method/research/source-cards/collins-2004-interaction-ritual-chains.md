core_claim: >
  Collins argues that ritual, not the isolated event, is sociology's proper unit of analysis for
  group cohesion. A successful interaction ritual requires four conditions in combination: bodily
  co-presence (a group gathering in one place, physical or increasingly mediated), a boundary that
  marks who is inside versus outside the interaction, a mutual focus of attention on the same
  object or activity, and a shared emotional mood. When these four conditions are met, the
  interaction generates "emotional energy" (EE) - a felt sense of confidence, enthusiasm, and
  belonging - along with symbols of group membership that persist after the interaction ends.
  Interactions that fail to meet these conditions do not produce this effect, regardless of how
  large or well-produced the gathering was.
summary: >
  This is a major work of micro-sociological theory building on Durkheim's earlier concept of
  "collective effervescence" (the heightened emotional state produced by group ritual, particularly
  religious ritual). Collins extends and generalizes it to ordinary social and organizational life,
  arguing that emotional energy generated in successful rituals is what motivates people to seek
  out further interaction with the same group, and that this - not the event's content or
  production value - is the actual mechanism producing durable group attachment. Crucially for this
  book's purposes, Collins distinguishes a single successful ritual (which produces a temporary EE
  boost) from a "ritual chain" - a repeated sequence of successful rituals with the same group over
  time, which is what produces durable, cumulative attachment and shared identity, rather than a
  one-off event alone.

limitations: >
  As with any grand sociological theory, the four-condition model is a simplifying framework, not a
  strict causal law - real interactions vary in degree on each condition rather than sharply meeting
  or failing to meet it, and Collins' own empirical grounding is drawn from a wide range of contexts
  Collins himself analyzed rather than from controlled organizational-event research specifically.
  Applying this framework to corporate or client events is an extension this book makes
  deliberately, not a direct finding from Collins' own case material.

  academic standing, not the only possible explanation for why some events build lasting connection
  and others don't.
