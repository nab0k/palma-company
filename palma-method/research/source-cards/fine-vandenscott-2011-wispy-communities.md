core_claim: >
  Not all socially meaningful affiliation takes the form of durable, institutionalized community. Fine
  and van den Scott propose "wispy communities" as a distinct sociological category: identities and
  attachments that are real and often intense while active, but that are explicitly transient and
  temporally bounded, tied to the existence of a gathering or occasion rather than to an ongoing
  organization. The article builds a typology to distinguish these forms rather than treating them as
  failed or immature versions of durable community. Wispy communities are real and can be affectively
  significant to participants without being durable, institutionalizable, or a stepping-stone to a
  "fuller" community.
summary: >
  Fine and van den Scott (drawing explicitly on Benedict Anderson's 1983 concept of "imagined
  communities," extended to a "mesolevel" below the nation-state) distinguish two basic forms of local
  affiliation: the **club form** (ongoing, scheduled "meetings" with an assumed core group and
  continuity across sessions - their examples include a weekly poker game, a chess club, a dramatic
  society, a fantasy-gaming group, the Sherlockian "Baker Street Irregulars," and Fine's own earlier
  fieldwork on a mushroom-collecting society) and the **occasion form** (rare, memorable, temporally
  bounded assemblies). The occasion form is further split into the **event** (a brief gathering
  organized around a performance, with no expectation of lasting ties among audience members - e.g.,
  a concert or spectacle) and the **gathering** (liminal, less frequent, organized around a shared
  interest, generating "interactional glue" that can persist as latent memory between occasions).
  Gatherings are further divided by scale and institutional control into **"nations"** (macro-
  gatherings such as Woodstock, August 1969, Bethel NY, roughly 500,000 attendees; and Burning Man,
  Black Rock Desert NV, since 1986, roughly 50,000 participants by 2009) and **"fests"** (micro-
  gatherings such as Lebowski Fest, a fan gathering for the film "The Big Lebowski," begun in 2002 in
  Louisville KY and held in 14 cities by 2009; chess tournaments; and Fine's own prior fieldwork on
  folk-art collectors, chess players, mushroomers, and fantasy gamers). The article's argument is that
  these transient forms should be studied on their own terms, not treated as an early or immature stage
  of a durable community that should be cultivated toward permanence. After a gathering ends, ties
  "slip into latent memory, available to be retrieved under proper circumstances" if the gathering
  recurs.
