core_claim: >
  Rational, self-interested individuals will not act to achieve shared group interests unless the
  group is small or a "selective incentive" (positive or negative, available only to contributors)
  compels participation - because any individual can free-ride on a public/collective good that
  others provide. Larger groups organize for common interests less effectively than small ones.
summary: >
  Olson's free-rider logic is the direct theoretical counterpoint to Ostrom's empirical finding
  that groups often do self-organize successfully. Olson predicted that collective goods (a
  professional community's shared reputation, a stakeholder network's shared trust, a public
  resource) would be systematically under-provided because rational actors prefer to let others
  bear the cost while still enjoying the benefit; the exceptions are small groups (where each
  member's contribution is individually visible and consequential) or groups that offer selective
  incentives - private benefits (status, access, material reward, or threat of exclusion) available
  only to those who participate. Olson's framework predicts that concentrated, well-organized
  minority interests (e.g., an industry lobby) will often dominate diffuse majority interests
  precisely because the minority group is small enough to overcome the free-rider problem.
key_evidence: >
  Primarily a theoretical/formal-economics argument; subsequently tested across decades of
  political-economy and public-goods research (interest-group formation, union membership,
  environmental collective action), with substantial empirical support for the core free-rider
  prediction alongside well-documented exceptions (Ostrom's commons research being the most
  prominent).
important_quote: >
  "Unless the number of individuals in a group is quite small, or unless there is coercion or some
  other special device to make individuals act in their common interest, rational, self-interested
  individuals will not act to achieve their common or group interests."

limitations: >
  Assumes a narrowly rational, self-interested actor model that later behavioral and experimental
  research (and Ostrom's own field evidence) shows is incomplete - real groups routinely
  under-perform or over-perform Olson's prediction depending on norms, monitoring, and identity, not
  just group size and incentive structure.
counterarguments: >
  Ostrom (1990) directly answers Olson: her eight design principles (boundaries, graduated
  sanctions, monitoring, etc.) explain the empirically common cases where groups do overcome the
  free-rider problem without external coercion - the two works are best read together, not as a
  refutation of one by the other but as a map of the conditions under which each prediction holds.
