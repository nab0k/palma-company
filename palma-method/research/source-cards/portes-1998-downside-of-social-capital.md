core_claim: >
  "Sociability cuts both ways." Portes documents four systematic negative consequences of social
  capital that the field's boosters (implicitly including Coleman and Putnam) tend to underweight:
  exclusion of outsiders, excess claims on group members, restrictions on individual freedom, and
  downward-levelling norms (group pressure that suppresses individual achievement to maintain group
  solidarity).
summary: >
  Portes' review is the most-cited critical counterpoint to the largely celebratory framing of
  social capital in Coleman and Putnam. He argues social capital scholarship had, by the late 1990s,
  become conceptually overstretched - applied so broadly it risked becoming synonymous with "all
  things good in social life," obscuring that the same mechanisms producing benefit for insiders
  (dense ties, strong norms, mutual obligation) systematically produce cost for outsiders and for
  individuals within the group who diverge from its norms. Strong bonding ties, in particular, can
  produce in-group favoritism and reduced information flow to non-members, concentrating benefit
  within a tightly-knit circle while limiting participation for anyone outside it - directly
  relevant to any organization designing a "community" that functions, in practice, as a closed
  circle around existing power.

  empirical specialty), ethnic economic-niche research, and prior sociological case studies of
  tight-knit groups exhibiting each of the four negative mechanisms.
