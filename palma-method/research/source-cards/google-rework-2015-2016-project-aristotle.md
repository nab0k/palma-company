core_claim: >
  This is Google's own primary account of Project Aristotle, confirming and superseding the
  secondary-source-only status previously flagged in VI-003. Google's People Analytics team
  studied 180 teams (115 engineering project teams and 65 sales pods) and, using over 35
  statistical models, found that team composition mattered far less than team dynamics. In order
  of importance, the five dynamics that predicted team effectiveness were: (1) psychological
  safety, (2) dependability, (3) structure and clarity, (4) meaning, and (5) impact. Psychological
  safety was explicitly the strongest and most important factor. The page explicitly credits Amy
  Edmondson's construct of "team psychological safety" as the foundation for this finding, and
  cites her original definition directly.
summary: >
  Following the earlier "Project Oxygen" study (what makes a good manager, released 2011),
  Google's People Analytics team ran Project Aristotle to answer "what makes a team effective at
  Google?" The research combined double-blind leadership interviews, existing engagement-survey
  data (250+ items) and Google's longitudinal "gDNA" study, and over 35 statistical models
  applied to hundreds of variables across the 180 studied teams. The five dynamics identified
  (psychological safety, dependability, structure and clarity, meaning, impact) are presented in
  explicit rank order of importance. Notably, several variables that many managers intuitively
  assume matter were found NOT to significantly predict effectiveness at Google: colocation,
  consensus-driven decision-making, extroversion, individual performance, workload size,
  seniority, team size, and tenure.
key_evidence: >

  ranged from 3 to 50 members (median 9).
