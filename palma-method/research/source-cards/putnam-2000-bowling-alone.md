core_claim: >
  Social capital - "connections among individuals: social networks and the norms of reciprocity
  and trustworthiness that arise from them" - had declined significantly in American civic life
  over the late 20th century, with measurable costs to democratic participation, economic
  performance, health, and happiness. Putnam distinguishes bonding capital (ties among similar
  people, "sociological superglue") from bridging capital (ties across difference,
  "sociological WD-40").
summary: >
  Building on but diverging from Coleman, Putnam treats social capital primarily as a collective/
  community-level property rather than an individual asset, and popularized the bonding/bridging
  distinction that later research (and Palma's own vocabulary) draws on heavily. Bonding capital
  is good for "getting by" (emotional support, mutual aid within a like group); bridging capital
  is necessary for "getting ahead" and for solving problems that require cooperation across
  difference (echoing and extending Granovetter's weak-ties argument to the civic/community level).
  Putnam links the decline of bridging associational life in America (bowling leagues, civic clubs,
  unions, PTAs) to generational change and, notably, television/media consumption patterns -
  a precursor concern to today's platform-fatigue and algorithmic-attention arguments in Track B.
key_evidence: >
  Extensive quantitative synthesis: General Social Survey trends, DDB Needham Life Style survey,
  membership records of civic associations (Elks, Rotary, PTA, unions, bowling leagues) showing
  multi-decade membership decline even as individual bowling participation rose ("bowling alone").
important_quote: >
  "Bonding social capital constitutes a kind of sociological superglue whereas bridging social
  capital provides a sociological WD-40."
quote_page_or_location: "Bowling Alone, ch. 22 (Simon & Schuster, 2000)"
methodology: "large-scale synthesis of survey and membership-record time series"

limitations: >
  Criticized for a somewhat nostalgic framing of mid-century associational life and for

  Later scholars (and Putnam's own later work, e.g. "E Pluribus Unum," 2007) show that ethnic and
  social diversity can, in the short run, reduce both bonding and bridging capital ("hunkering
  down" effect) - complicating any simple story that more diversity automatically produces more
  bridging capital.
