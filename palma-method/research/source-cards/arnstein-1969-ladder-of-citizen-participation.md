core_claim: >
  Citizen/stakeholder "participation" is not one thing; it ranges across a typology - Arnstein's
  eight-rung ladder - from manipulation and therapy (non-participation, dressed up as involvement),
  through informing, consultation, and placation (degrees of tokenism, where people are heard but
  have no guaranteed power to affect the outcome), up to partnership, delegated power, and citizen
  control (degrees of actual citizen power over decisions and outcomes). The distinction that
  matters is not whether people were asked, but whether they had any actual power to change the
  result.
summary: >
  Arnstein, writing about U.S. federal urban renewal and anti-poverty programs, argued that the word
  "participation" was being used to legitimize decisions already made, without redistributing any
  real power to the people ostensibly being included - a pattern she called "manipulation" and
  "therapy" at the ladder's bottom rungs. The middle rungs (informing, consultation, placation)
  involve real listening but no binding power: officials can hear objections and proceed unchanged.
  Only the top three rungs (partnership, delegated power, citizen control) involve an actual
  transfer of decision-making authority. Arnstein's central warning, directly relevant to this
  book's chapter on Listen, is that going through the motions of consultation without any
  possibility of the outcome changing produces predictable stakeholder cynicism and disengagement -
  worse, in her account, than not consulting at all, because it creates false expectations and then
  breaks them.
