core_claim: >
  Effective leadership decision-making depends on first correctly diagnosing which of several
  different types of context a situation falls into - simple/clear, complicated, complex, chaotic,
  or disorder (context unclear) - because each context calls for a fundamentally different
  decision-making approach, and applying the wrong approach (e.g., seeking a single best-practice
  answer in a genuinely complex, unpredictable situation) is a common and costly leadership error.
summary: >
  The Cynefin framework sorts situations into five domains. In "simple/clear" contexts, cause and
  effect are obvious to everyone, and the right approach is to sense, categorize, and apply known
  best practice. In "complicated" contexts, cause and effect exist but require expertise to diagnose
  - the approach is sense, analyze, apply good practice (there may be multiple valid answers). In
  "complex" contexts, cause and effect are only clear in retrospect, patterns emerge rather than
  being predictable in advance, and the right approach is to probe (run small, safe-to-fail
  experiments), sense what happens, and respond - not to plan a detailed solution up front. In
  "chaotic" contexts, there is no discernible relationship between cause and effect at all, and the
  priority is to act immediately to stabilize the situation, then sense and respond. A fifth
  "disorder" domain describes situations where it is not yet clear which of the other four domains
  applies at all. Snowden and Boone's central warning for leaders is that most organizational
  training and instinct pushes people toward complicated-domain thinking (find the expert, find the
  best practice) even when a situation is actually complex - where that approach fails, because
  complex situations do not have a discoverable "right answer" in advance, only patterns that can be
  probed for and sensed as they emerge.
