core_claim: >
  Boundaries operate at two distinct, related levels that should not be conflated: **symbolic
  boundaries** (conceptual distinctions people make to categorize objects, people, and practices -
  the "insider/outsider" and "us/them" distinctions people carry in their heads) and **social
  boundaries** (objectified forms of social difference manifested in unequal access to resources and
  social opportunities - the actual patterns of association, exclusion, and inequality that
  materialize when symbolic boundaries are widely agreed upon and acted on). Symbolic boundaries are
  a necessary but not sufficient condition for social boundaries to form.
summary: >
  Lamont and Molnár's field-defining review synthesizes boundary scholarship across sociology,
  anthropology, and social psychology into a coherent framework, showing that boundary processes
  explain group formation, identity, inequality, and inclusion/exclusion across enormously varied
  contexts (ethnicity, class, gender, profession, and small-group settings). Critically, boundaries
  are not simply barriers to be removed - they are also the mechanism by which any group, however
  small or informal, becomes recognizable as a group at all. This is directly relevant to Research
  Question 3 (how boundaries, bridges, insiders, outsiders, and intermediaries shape access,
  knowledge, conflict, and coordination) because it supplies a rigorous vocabulary for talking about
  boundaries as constitutive, not just exclusionary.
