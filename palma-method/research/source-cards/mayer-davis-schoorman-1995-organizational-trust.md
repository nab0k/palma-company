core_claim: >
  Trust is "the willingness of a party to be vulnerable to the actions of another party based on
  the expectation that the other will perform a particular action important to the trustor,
  irrespective of the ability to monitor or control that other party." Trust in a specific
  relationship is predicted by the trustor's perception of the trustee's ability, benevolence, and
  integrity (the "ABI model"), combined with the trustor's own general propensity to trust.
summary: >
  The most widely cited management definition of trust, distinguishing trust (a willingness to
  accept vulnerability) from its antecedents (ability = competence in the relevant domain;
  benevolence = genuine care for the trustor's interests beyond self-interest; integrity =
  adherence to principles the trustor finds acceptable) and from behavioral risk-taking (the actual
  act of relying on the other party). Trust is relationship- and context-specific: a person can be
  trusted for financial judgment (ability) but not for candor (integrity), and the three factors
  combine rather than substitute for each other.
key_evidence: >
  Theoretical/integrative model synthesizing prior empirical trust research across psychology,
  sociology, and economics; later validated and extended by hundreds of subsequent empirical
  studies (the model remains the dominant framework in organizational trust research three decades
  on).

limitations: >
  Model is dyadic (trustor-trustee) and was developed primarily for intra-organizational contexts
  (e.g. manager-subordinate); applying it to organization-level or brand-level trust (a company
  being "trusted" by a stakeholder network) requires an extension the original paper does not fully
  cover.

  transactional relationships where benevolence cannot yet be assessed.
