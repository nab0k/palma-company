core_claim: >
  Embedded (trust-based, relationally close) ties produce real economic advantages - trust,
  fine-grained information transfer, resource access, and integrative problem-solving - but only up
  to a threshold. Beyond that threshold, "over-embeddedness" reduces adaptability, insulates firms
  from information outside the network, and can derail performance, especially under external shocks.
summary: >
  Based on ethnographic fieldwork and interviews across 23 firms in the New York City women's
  better-dress apparel industry, Uzzi distinguishes "arm's-length" ties (market-based, transactional)
  from "embedded" ties (trust, fine-grained information exchange, joint problem-solving). Firms with
  a healthy mix of both types outperformed firms relying on either exclusively. Purely arm's-length
  networks lacked the trust needed for complex coordination; purely embedded networks became
  vulnerable because embeddedness raises the cost of screening the (mixed-quality, redundant)
  information circulating inside the network, crowding out exposure to genuinely new information and
  reducing adaptive capacity when the environment changed. This is the most rigorous empirical
  demonstration in this literature map that strong-tie density is not simply "more is better" - it
  is a resource with a documented tipping point.
key_evidence: >
  Ethnographic interviews with CEOs/executives of 23 apparel-industry firms (manufacturers,
  contractors, designers) plus network-structure analysis of their transaction relationships.
important_quote: >
  "Embeddedness... has a curvilinear effect on economic performance: positive up to a threshold,
  after which the same features that made embeddedness valuable begin to undermine performance."
quote_page_or_location: "Uzzi, 1997, summary of core finding (ASQ 42:35-67)"
methodology: "ethnographic fieldwork with structured interviews and network analysis"
sample: "23 firms, New York City women's better-dress apparel industry"
limitations: >
  Single industry, single geography, mid-1990s data - the specific threshold at which embeddedness
  becomes harmful is industry- and context-dependent and was not established as a general numeric
  rule; applying the finding to Palma's client contexts (professional services, executive networks)
  requires treating it as a structural warning, not a literal transferable threshold.
counterarguments: >
  Some subsequent network research argues the "paradox" is less about embeddedness itself and more
  about lack of network diversity - a firm can maintain deep, embedded ties with a diverse enough
  set of partners to avoid the insulation effect, suggesting the fix is diversifying embedded ties
  rather than limiting embeddedness per se.
