  Communication measurement and evaluation, as a matter of cross-industry professional consensus,

summary: >
  The Barcelona Principles originated in 2010 as an industry-wide consensus statement (led by AMEC,
  with input from major PR and communication-measurement bodies globally) responding to widespread,
  poor measurement practice in public relations and communication - most notably the continued use
  of Advertising Value Equivalency (treating earned media mentions as if they had the same value as
  equivalent paid advertising space), which the Principles explicitly reject as invalid. The 2020
  "3.0" revision sharpened the framework's focus on inclusion of all relevant channels (earned,
  owned, shared, paid) under one consistent measurement approach, extended applicability to
  non-commercial objectives (reputation, stakeholder engagement, societal impact, not just sales),
  and reinforced that setting SMART (specific, measurable, actionable, relevant, time-bound) goals
  is a precondition for meaningful measurement, not an optional add-on. The framework explicitly
  separates outputs, outcomes, and (where feasible) broader organizational or societal impact as
  three different, non-interchangeable levels of measurement.
