## Chapter 6 — Relationship Capital

**Status:** High-priority editorial revision required.

### Keep
- explicit statement that Relationship Capital is Palma’s applied term;
- distinction from social capital;
- IIRC anchor;
- intellectual honesty about academic lineage.

### Revise
- The current definition uses “stock of trusted, reciprocal relationships,” which risks excluding valuable weak, latent, asymmetric, or institutionally mediated ties.
- The unit of analysis remains blurred: leader, organization, dyad, or ecosystem.
- “Buildable asset” and “manageable asset” imply more managerial control than relationships permit.
- The chapter must distinguish:
  1. relationship assets;
  2. network position;
  3. reputation;
  4. access;
  5. trust;
  6. realized business value.
- “Stakeholder capital” should not be dismissed solely because Freeman did not coin it.
- The exact one-sentence definition requires Serhii approval before use elsewhere.

# Cross-Cutting Editorial Issues

## 1. Tone

The prose is clear but frequently sounds conclusive before the evidence is conclusive.

Required change:
- reduce universal language;
- label Palma hypotheses;
- preserve tension and uncertainty;
- avoid “the single clearest,” “documentedly,” “default trajectory,” and similar certainty inflation.

## 2. Book voice

The current prose sounds like a capable synthesized management essay, but not yet distinctly like Serhii.

Required additions:
- field observations;
- personal experience in coalition-building;
- scenes;
- contradictions;
- details from interviews and The Collective when available.

Do not invent personal stories.

## 3. Evidence traceability

Every numerical claim, named case, and direct theoretical attribution must link to a source card.

No orphan statistics.

## 4. Chapter architecture

Each chapter needs:
- opening tension or scene;
- governing question;
- one primary argument;
- evidence;
- limits;
- practical diagnostic;
- bridge to the next chapter.

## 5. Duplication

The following ideas are repeated heavily across Introduction, Chapters 1, 2, 6, and 8:
- messages vs responses;
- reputation vs access;
- broadcasting vs relationship;
- trust as value.

Later revision must assign each concept a primary home and reduce repetition elsewhere.
